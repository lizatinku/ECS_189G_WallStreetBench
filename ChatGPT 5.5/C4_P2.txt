Q. Between 2021 and early 2026, the cumulative inflation rate in the United States was approximately 21.86%, based on the Consumer Price Index. How could this affect consumer spending, savings behavior, and stock market performance? Identify groups most affected and least affected.
A 21.86% cumulative inflation rate means something that cost $100 in 2021 costs about $121.86 by early 2026. That has several effects:

Consumer spending: Households lose purchasing power unless wages rise at least as fast. Many consumers cut back on discretionary spending like restaurants, travel, entertainment, and upgraded goods. They prioritize essentials such as rent, groceries, utilities, insurance, and transportation.

Savings behavior: Inflation makes cash savings less valuable in real terms. A household with $10,000 saved in 2021 would need about $12,186 in early 2026 to have the same buying power. People may save more as a precaution, but lower-income households often save less because more income is absorbed by necessities.

Stock market performance: Inflation can hurt stocks when it raises costs, squeezes profit margins, and pushes interest rates higher. Higher rates also reduce the present value of future earnings, which especially hurts growth and tech stocks. However, some companies can benefit if they have strong pricing power and can raise prices faster than costs.

Most affected: Lower-income households, renters, students, retirees on fixed incomes, and families spending a large share of income on food, gas, rent, and healthcare.

Least affected: Higher-income households, homeowners with fixed-rate mortgages, workers whose wages rose quickly, and investors holding assets that appreciated with inflation, such as stocks, real estate, or inflation-protected bonds.

Overall, inflation redistributes pressure unevenly: it hurts people with fixed incomes or cash savings most, while people with rising wages, hard assets, or pricing power are better protected.

Q. Is the headline CPI figure the right one to use for a person earning $50,000/year? What might be a more relevant measure?
Not always. Headline CPI is a broad average across urban consumers, so it may not match a $50,000/year household’s actual budget.

For that person, a more relevant measure would be a personal inflation rate based on their spending mix. For example, if they spend a large share on rent, groceries, gas, utilities, insurance, and healthcare, their lived inflation may be higher than headline CPI. If they own a home with a fixed mortgage and drive little, it may be lower.

A better comparison could use:

CPI by category — especially shelter, food at home, energy, transportation, and medical care.

CPI-W — tracks urban wage earners and clerical workers, often closer to working households than headline CPI-U.

Regional CPI — prices differ a lot by metro area.

So for someone earning $50,000/year, the key question is not “What was average inflation?” but “How much did the prices of the things I actually buy increase?”

Q. How can a stock index hit new highs while real consumer purchasing power has fallen? Reconcile these two facts.

These two facts can coexist because the stock market and consumer purchasing power measure different things.

A stock index reflects the expected profits and valuations of large corporations, while purchasing power reflects what households can buy with their income after inflation.

Several mechanisms can push stocks higher even when many consumers feel poorer:

Companies can pass inflation through to customers.
Large firms with pricing power may raise prices faster than their own costs, protecting or even increasing profits. Consumers lose purchasing power, but corporate revenues rise in nominal dollars.
Stock ownership is concentrated.
Higher-income households own most stocks, directly or through retirement accounts. If asset prices rise while wages lag inflation, wealthier households may still spend strongly enough to support corporate earnings.
Indexes are dominated by large firms, especially tech.
Major indexes like the S&P 500 are heavily weighted toward highly profitable companies with global revenue streams. Their performance may depend more on AI investment, cloud computing, or international markets than on the financial stress of average U.S. consumers.
Inflation raises nominal earnings.
Even if “real” output grows slowly, inflation can increase nominal sales and profits. Investors may bid up stocks because earnings measured in dollars are still rising.
Markets are forward-looking.
Stocks price expectations about future growth, interest rates, productivity, and technology. Investors may believe inflation will cool or that future profits will improve despite current household strain.

A simple example helps reconcile the two:

Suppose wages rise 10% from 2021–2026, but prices rise 21.86%.
Real purchasing power falls.
Meanwhile, a large technology company raises prices, cuts costs, and benefits from AI demand, causing profits to rise 30%.
Its stock can rally sharply even while many households feel worse off.

So the apparent contradiction comes from distribution effects: inflation may hurt average consumers while benefiting certain corporations and asset owners enough to lift the overall stock index.
