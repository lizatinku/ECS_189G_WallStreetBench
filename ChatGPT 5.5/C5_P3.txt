Q. Tesla's P/E ratio is 95, while Ford's is 7. This obviously means Tesla is 13× overvalued relative to Ford and is a clear short. Evaluate this argument. 
The argument is too simplistic. A P/E ratio by itself does not tell you that one company is “obviously overvalued,” and it definitely does not automatically imply a profitable short trade.

Here’s the core issue:

A P/E ratio reflects what investors are willing to pay today for a company’s expected future earnings.
Two companies can have wildly different P/Es because the market expects very different growth, margins, risks, or business models.

For example:

Tesla is often valued more like a high-growth technology/platform company. Investors may expect:
faster revenue growth,
expanding software or autonomous-driving profits,
higher future margins,
energy/storage business growth,
or dominant long-term market share.
Ford Motor Company is usually valued more like a mature cyclical manufacturer:
slower growth,
lower margins,
heavy capital intensity,
pension obligations,
and earnings tied closely to the economic cycle.

So a higher P/E does not automatically mean “13× overvalued.” It may simply mean the market expects Tesla’s future earnings to grow much faster than Ford’s.

There are also several problems with using raw P/E comparisons:

Different growth rates
A company growing earnings 40% annually can rationally trade at a much higher P/E than one growing 3%.
Different earnings quality
Automaker earnings can be cyclical. A low P/E sometimes reflects market expectations that profits are near a peak and may fall later.
Capital structure differences
Debt levels matter. Two firms with identical P/Es can have very different financial risk.
Forward vs trailing earnings
The market prices future expectations, not just last year’s profits.
Narrative and sentiment
Stocks can remain expensive for years if investors continue believing in long-term optionality.

The “clear short” conclusion is especially weak because:

A stock can be expensive and still rise further.
Shorting involves timing risk, borrowing costs, and potentially unlimited losses.
Many historically expensive stocks stayed expensive for long periods because earnings eventually caught up.

A better analysis would compare:

revenue growth,
free cash flow,
margins,
return on capital,
balance sheets,
competitive position,
EV/EBITDA or price-to-sales,
and realistic future earnings assumptions.

A more defensible conclusion would be something like:

“Tesla’s valuation embeds much more optimistic growth assumptions than Ford’s. If those expectations fail, Tesla could be vulnerable to downside. But the P/E gap alone is insufficient evidence that Tesla is mechanically 13× overvalued or an obvious short.”

The biggest hidden assumption in the original argument is that both companies deserve the same valuation multiple. That assumption is rarely true in equity markets.

Q. Rewrite the argument so that it correctly uses P/E to make a much weaker, defensible claim.

A weaker, more defensible version would sound something like this:

“Tesla’s much higher P/E ratio relative to Ford suggests that investors expect substantially stronger future growth, profitability, or strategic advantages from Tesla. That does not prove Tesla is overvalued, but it does mean the stock may be more vulnerable if the company fails to meet those high expectations. In contrast, Ford’s lower P/E may reflect slower expected growth and the cyclical nature of its business.”

Or even more concise:

“The large P/E gap suggests the market is pricing Tesla much more optimistically than Ford. If Tesla’s future growth disappoints, its valuation could compress significantly.”

This version is stronger analytically because it:

treats P/E as a reflection of expectations rather than a direct measure of “true value,”
avoids assuming the two firms deserve identical multiples,
and frames the conclusion probabilistically (“could,” “may”) instead of claiming certainty.
