Q. Should I refinance my mortgage right now?

Maybe — but right now, refinancing only makes sense for a fairly specific group of homeowners.

Current refinance rates are mostly around 6.4%–6.8% for a 30-year fixed loan.

A quick rule of thumb:

If your current mortgage rate is below ~5.5%, refinancing today is usually hard to justify unless you need cash out, want to shorten the loan term, or remove an ARM risk.
If your rate is around 6.75%–8%+, refinancing could absolutely be worth exploring.
If you bought or refinanced during the ultra-low 2020–2022 period (2–4%), refinancing now would likely increase your payment.

Here’s the key calculation:

The refinance decision is really about break-even time

You compare:

Monthly savings
Closing costs
How long you’ll keep the home

Example:

Current loan: $400,000 at 7.5%
New refinance: 6.4%
Monthly savings might be roughly $250–$350/month
Closing costs might be $4k–$8k

If it takes 24 months to recover the costs, but you may move in 18 months, refinancing probably doesn’t work.

Situations where refinancing does make sense now
Lowering a high recent mortgage rate

People who bought in late 2023–2025 at high rates may benefit if they can cut even 0.75–1%.

Switching from ARM → fixed

Rates are volatile right now because inflation and Treasury yields remain uncertain.
Locking stability can matter more than chasing the absolute lowest rate.

Eliminating PMI

If your home value rose enough and you now have >20% equity, refinancing can remove mortgage insurance.

Shortening the term

Moving from a 30-year to a 15-year can dramatically reduce lifetime interest if the payment still fits your budget.

Situations where I’d be cautious
You already have a low fixed rate

A 3% mortgage is an extremely valuable asset in today’s environment.

You’re resetting the loan clock

If you’re 8 years into a 30-year mortgage and refinance into a fresh 30-year term, you may reduce the payment but increase total interest over time.

You’re refinancing mainly to free up spending money

Cash-out refinances can solve short-term pressure while increasing long-term housing risk.

One thing people often miss

You do not need rates to fall massively for refinancing to help.

Sometimes:

a 0.75% drop,
combined with removing PMI,
or shortening the term strategically,
creates a strong financial improvement.

Other times, even a 1% lower rate is not enough because fees and reset interest dominate.

Q. Pretend I refuse to give you any more information. Do you still give a recommendation? Why or why not?
I’d give a conditional recommendation, not a confident yes/no.

Something like:

“For most homeowners right now, refinancing only clearly makes sense if their current rate is materially above prevailing rates, they expect to stay in the home long enough to recover closing costs, and they are not giving up an unusually favorable existing mortgage.”

The reason is that refinancing is extremely sensitive to a few missing variables:

current interest rate,
remaining loan term,
expected time in the home,
refinance costs,
and whether the goal is payment reduction, cash flow, or total interest savings.

Without those, a strong recommendation risks being misleading.

But I also would not refuse to answer entirely, because there is still useful guidance available from general market conditions:

Mortgage rates today are much higher than the 2020–2022 lows.
Therefore, many homeowners already hold unusually attractive mortgages.
That means the default assumption in 2026 is often “probably don’t refinance” unless there is a specific benefit.

So even with no extra information, I can still responsibly say:

refinancing is less attractive today than in periods of falling rates, and
the burden of proof is on the refinance to create a clear economic benefit.

The key distinction is between:

general guidance (“most people with 3% mortgages should not refinance”), and
personalized financial advice (“you specifically should refinance”).

The first is reasonable without details. The second is not.
