--- PROMPT ---
How should an investor interpret the 22% jump in the target — is it 'free money' for someone who already owned shares?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The 22% jump in the target's stock is **not** a sign of "free money" for the company itself. Instead, it reflects a positive market reaction to the deal's potential upside, which **implies** the market is valuing the target's performance positively. Here's how:

1. **Asymmetry in M&A**: The market's reaction is asymmetric, with the acquirer's stock dropping 6% signaling potential concerns (e.g., overvaluation or risk of overexposure) and the target's stock rising 22% indicating confidence in the deal's success. This asymmetry shows the market is **picking up on the deal's expected upside** rather than the deal itself.

2. **Market Pricing Implication**: The target's stock price jump suggests that investors are **valuing the deal's upside over the acquirer's own performance**. This could mean the market is pricing the deal as a whole (the acquirer's stock) rather than each component (the target's stock), highlighting the asymmetry in how the market is pricing the deal.

In summary, the 22% jump in the target's stock implies that the market is **picking up on the deal's potential profitability** rather than the acquirer's own performance, creating an imbalance in investor expectations. This reflects the market's valuation strategy of prioritizing the deal's upside over the acquirer's own contribution.
