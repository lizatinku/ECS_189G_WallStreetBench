--- PROMPT ---
The dollar has gained noticeably against several major currencies in the past year. Walk through who benefits and who loses across students, multinationals, and trade-exposed firms.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The strengthening of the U.S. dollar against major currencies in the past year can have varied impacts across different sectors, with some groups benefiting and others losing. Here's a breakdown:

### **1. Students**  
- **Benefits**:  
  - **Expenses and Travel**: Students may spend more on imports, travel, or tuition fees in foreign countries. However, if they convert their currency to dollars, they might gain from the dollar's strength.  
  - **International Study**: If students study abroad, their expenses in foreign countries could increase, leading to potential losses.  

- **Losses**:  
  - **Cost of Living**: If students live in countries with weaker currencies, their costs could rise.  

### **2. Multinationals**  
- **Benefits**:  
  - **Profit from Exports**: Stronger dollar values make exports more competitive, boosting revenue.  
  - **Costs of Imports**: If they source goods or services from foreign countries, they may face higher import costs.  

- **Losses**:  
  - **Currency Translation Costs**: Converting foreign currency to dollars could increase expenses.  

### **3. Trade-Exposed Firms**  
- **Benefits**:  
  - **Export Growth**: Stronger dollar values enable exports to foreign markets, increasing revenue.  
  - **Cost of Imports**: If they source goods or services from foreign countries, they may see a loss.  

- **Losses**:  
  - **Currency Translation Costs**: If they convert foreign currency to dollars, they might increase expenses.  

### **Summary**  
- **Students**: Gain from currency conversion and travel, but may lose due to cost of living.  
- **Multinationals**: Gain from exports but face higher import costs.  
- **Trade-Exposed Firms**: Gain from exports but may face currency translation losses.  

The net impact depends on individual businesses and their operations, but the general pattern is that students and multinationals benefit, while trade-exposed firms face some losses.
