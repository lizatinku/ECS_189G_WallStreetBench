### **Why It's Not Directly Comparable:**
- The CPI is a **percentage of the average consumer's cost** (not your personal income). For example, if someone

- This means the CPI figure doesn't capture **your personal purchasing power** directly. It's a measure of 
inflation for the whole economy, not for a specific individual.

relevant measure. However, if you're looking for a **more accurate measure of your personal spending power**, 
you might consider:

- **Personal consumption expenditure** (not CPI), which tracks how much you spend on goods and services 
directly.
- **Inflation-adjusted personal income taxes** or **consumer price index adjustments** if you're 
analyzing the impact of inflation on your financial situation.

### **Conclusion:**
- The headline CPI figure is **not the right measure** for a personal income of $50,000. It reflects the 
**average price of goods and services** in the U.S., not your personal spending.
- A more relevant measure would be **personal consumption expenditures**, which track how much you spend on 
goods and services directly.
