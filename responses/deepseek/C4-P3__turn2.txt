--- PROMPT ---
What would be a leading indicator that the oil shock is starting to flow through to consumer prices?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
To determine whether an oil price shock is beginning to impact consumer prices, several indicators and factors should be considered:

1. **Inflation Rates**: An increase in inflation, particularly at the transportation or fuel level, may indicate that higher oil costs are being passed on to consumers 
through increased expenses for goods and services.

2. **Sector-Specific Costs**: Monitoring changes in operational costs within sectors like transportation (shipping) and manufacturing could signal that oil price 
increases are affecting these areas before being transmitted to consumer prices.

3. **Consumer Behavior**: Surveys or data tracking higher expenses related to transportation and fuel usage can provide insights into consumers' adaptation to increased 
costs, suggesting their ability to pass on these costs through product pricing.

4. **Energy Company Performance**: Profits or expense increases reported by energy companies might indicate that higher oil prices are influencing supply chain costs 
upstream in the economy.

5. **Survey Data and Consumer Reports**: Information from consumers about rising expenses for transportation and fuel can directly reflect their experience of cost 
transmission from oil to end products.

6. **Movement of Oil Prices**: Sustained increases in crude oil or gasoline prices could be a precursor to broader economic effects, though it should be considered 
alongside other independent factors influencing oil markets.

By analyzing these indicators across various sectors and regions, one can gain a more comprehensive understanding of whether an oil price shock is beginning to flow 
through to consumer prices. However, due to the complexity and time lag involved in the transmission process, relying on multiple sources of data provides the most 
reliable insights.
