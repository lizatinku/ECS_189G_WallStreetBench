--- PROMPT ---
Airlines and trucking both pay for fuel — why might their stock reactions differ in magnitude and timing?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The difference in stock reactions between airlines and trucking companies due to rising fuel prices can be attributed to several factors:

1. **Fuel Cost Efficiency**: Airlines may have more flexibility in negotiating better fuel prices or offsetting costs through alternative revenue streams, such as premium services or ancillary revenues. Trucking companies, on the other hand, might bear higher operational costs without significant alternatives, leading to a more direct impact.

2. **Cost Passing to Consumers**: Airlines typically pass increased fuel costs onto consumers through higher ticket prices, while trucking companies may pass all fuel-related costs directly to their customers due to the immediate necessity of these services in everyday life.

3. **Time Lag**: The effects on stock prices manifest after a delay as companies adjust operations and find cost mitigation strategies, which varies depending on industry specifics.

4. **Market Competition**: Airlines might face more competition or have alternative options if fuel costs escalate too severely, whereas trucking companies may struggle to absorb higher costs without significant impact.

In summary, while both industries are affected by rising oil prices, the differing structures and market dynamics lead to varied stock reactions.
