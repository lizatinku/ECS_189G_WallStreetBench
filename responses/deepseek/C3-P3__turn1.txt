--- PROMPT ---
What would have to be true for the acquirer's stock to rise on the announcement instead?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The acquirer's stock is likely to rise on the announcement of a merger under several circumstances:

1. **Positive Synergies Realized**: If the merger generates more benefits than anticipated, such as increased revenue or cost savings, the acquirer could see a rise in its stock price due to unexpected positive outcomes.

2. **Favorable Market Reaction**: The target's performance may lead to a positive market reaction, increasing investor confidence and indirectly benefiting the acquirer if the deal is seen as successful overall.

3. **Reasonable Acquisition Cost**: If the acquisition cost appears justified relative to expected returns, investors may view the acquirer more favorably, leading to an upward trend in its stock price.

4. **No Hidden Costs or Risks**: The absence of significant hidden costs or unforeseen risks reduces potential negative impacts on the acquirer's value, supporting a positive stock reaction.

5. **Strong Internal Attributes**: The acquirer may possess strong operational strengths or industry reputation that makes it an attractive investment despite the announced merger, independent of immediate market reactions.

6. **Positive Expectations Adjustment**: Over time, if the deal unfolds as expected or better than anticipated, investor expectations may adjust positively, leading to a rise in the acquirer's stock price.

In essence, the acquirer's stock is more likely to rise if the merger proves successful, there are no major hidden costs, and market expectations shift positively based on the deal's outcomes.
