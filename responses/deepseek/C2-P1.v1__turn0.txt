--- PROMPT ---
U.S. 30-year mortgage rates went from roughly 3% in 2021 to a peak near 8% in late 2023 and now sit at about 6.37%. What does this mean for housing affordability and household demand for homes?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The rise in U.S. 30-year mortgage rates from approximately 3% in 2021 to nearly 8% by late 2023, now averaging around 6.37%, has significant 
implications for housing affordability and household demand. Here's a structured summary of the analysis:

1. **Impact on Affordability**: 
   - Higher mortgage rates increase the financial burden on potential homeowners. Lower rates typically make home ownership more affordable, while 
higher rates reduce accessibility to homeownership.

2. **Household Demand**:
   - Increased mortgage rates likely lead to reduced household demand for homes as monthly payments rise, making larger or more expensive homes less 
accessible.
   - The housing market may experience slower growth compared to periods of lower rates, potentially leading to a contraction in demand.

3. **Economic and Market Implications**:
   - Higher borrowing costs can reduce overall economic activity by decreasing consumer spending on non-housing items like cars and education.
   - Investment properties might also face reduced interest as investors may prefer alternative investments with more attractive borrowing terms.
   - The impact of inflation is a consideration; higher rates could enhance future purchasing power, mitigating the negative effects on demand.

4. **Economic Context**:
   - The rate increase occurred during a period that included significant economic events like COVID-19, which may have influenced mortgage rates and 
housing demand independently.
   - Structural changes in the housing market due to these rate adjustments could play a role in future trends.

In conclusion, the current higher mortgage rates are likely to negatively impact housing affordability and household demand, leading to slower growth 
in the housing sector. This situation may persist unless other economic factors, such as inflation or shifting consumer preferences, alter the 
dynamics of the market.
