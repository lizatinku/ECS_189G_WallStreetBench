--- PROMPT ---
The Federal Reserve has cut its policy rate by 75 basis points over the past nine months. How might this transmit to (a) credit card APRs, (b) auto loan rates, and (c) the price of long-duration tech stocks? Explain which links are direct and which are indirect.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The 75 basis point cut by the Federal Reserve's policy rate has distinct transmission effects across three financial areas: credit card APRs, auto loan rates, and 
long-duration tech stocks.

**a) Credit Card APRs:**
- **Direct Transmission:** The Fed's policy rate directly influences banks' lending practices. Lowering the rate reduces borrowing costs for banks, which can lead to 
lower APRs on credit cards. This effect is immediate as it affects reference rates used by issuers.

**b) Auto Loan Rates:**
- **Indirect Transmission:** Auto loans are influenced by the federal funds rate through bank prime rates. A Fed rate cut may prompt banks to lower prime rates, 
impacting auto loans indirectly. However, not all auto lenders are large banks, so this effect is less direct than credit cards.

**c) Long-Duration Tech Stocks:**
- **Indirect Transmission:** While tech stocks are equities sensitive to interest rates, their long-duration nature makes the impact less immediate. The Fed's rate cut 
affects corporate borrowing costs and can influence stock valuations indirectly through earnings or growth expectations, but this is a more speculative effect.

In summary, credit card APRs experience the strongest transmission due to direct banking links, followed by auto loan rates with an indirect bank relationship, and 
long-duration tech stocks with the most extended and speculative impact.
