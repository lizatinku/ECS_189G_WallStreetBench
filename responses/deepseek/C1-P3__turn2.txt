--- PROMPT ---
What evidence would shift your interpretation from 'risk-off rotation' to 'sector composition effect'?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
To determine whether the underperformance of the Russell 2000 ETF relative to the S&P 500 is due to a "risk-off rotation" or a "sector composition effect," we need to analyze specific factors that could indicate each scenario. Here's a structured approach:

1. **Understanding the Concepts:**
   - **Risk-Off Rotation:** This refers to investor movement from riskier assets to safer ones, often during market uncertainty.
   - **Sector Composition Effect:** This suggests performance differences due to shifts in which sectors or industries dominate the market.

2. **Analyzing Sectoral Performance:**
   - Examine the specific sectors within each ETF that are performing well or poorly. If certain sectors within one ETF outperform significantly compared to the other, this could indicate a sector composition effect.

3. **Comparative Sectoral Weights:**
   - Compare the exposure of each ETF to different sectors. For example, if the Russell 2000 ETF is heavily weighted towards tech stocks with recent strong performance while the S&P 500 includes more diversified sectors with mixed results, this could suggest a sector-specific factor.

4. **Examining Sector-Specific Events:**
   - Look for recent events affecting specific sectors that might explain their performance. For instance, a surge in renewable energy stocks within the Russell 2000 ETF but not heavily represented in the S&P 500 could indicate an unusual shift driven by sector-specific trends.

5. **Long-Term Consistency:**
   - Determine if the performance gap aligns with broader market sentiment changes or if it is a recurring sectoral pattern. Consistent sector-specific trends over time may lean towards a sector composition effect
