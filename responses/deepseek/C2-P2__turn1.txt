--- PROMPT ---
Which of these three pass-throughs typically lags the most, and why?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
In analyzing the lag effect of the Federal Reserve's 75 basis point policy rate cut across credit card APRs, auto loan rates, and long-duration tech stocks, we can 
summarize the findings as follows:

1. **Credit Card APRs:** These are expected to experience the shortest lag due to their direct relationship with banks' lending practices. Credit cards are closely tied 
to the Fed's policy rate through bank prime rates, allowing for a relatively quick adjustment in APRs.

2. **Auto Loan Rates:** Auto loans are influenced by the Fed's rate cut but experience a slightly longer lag compared to credit cards. This is because auto lenders need 
time to adjust their terms and communicate the new interest rates to customers, often through updated financing agreements with manufacturers.

3. **Long-Duration Tech Stocks:** These stocks typically exhibit the longest lag due to their sensitivity to broader economic conditions rather than direct changes in 
the Fed's policy rate. Their valuation is influenced by corporate profitability, investor sentiment, and expectations about future rate changes, which take time to 
materialize.

In conclusion, while credit card APRs are most directly affected with minimal lag, long-duration tech stocks experience the longest lag due to their indirect 
relationship with Fed policy changes and broader market dynamics.
