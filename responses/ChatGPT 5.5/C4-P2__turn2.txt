--- PROMPT ---
How can a stock index hit new highs while real consumer purchasing power has fallen? Reconcile these two facts.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Yes—these two facts are completely compatible.

A **stock index high is usually a nominal asset-price fact**, while falling purchasing power is a **real consumption fact**.

## Stock market information for SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust (SPY)

* SPDR S&P 500 ETF Trust is a fund in the USA market.
* The price is 753.2 USD currently with a change of 2.74 USD (0.00%) from the previous close.
* The latest open price was 750.21 USD and the intraday volume is 11523419.
* The intraday high is 753.65 USD and the intraday low is 748.2 USD.
* The latest trade time is Thursday, May 28, 08:00:33 PDT.

Suppose prices rose **21.86%**. Then:

[
\text{real stock return} = \frac{1+\text{nominal return}}{1+0.2186}-1
]

So if an index rose **25% nominally**, the real gain is only:

[
\frac{1.25}{1.2186}-1 \approx 2.6%
]

If it rose **15% nominally**, then in real terms it actually **lost purchasing power**:

[
\frac{1.15}{1.2186}-1 \approx -5.6%
]

So the first reconciliation is simple: **a nominal high is not necessarily a real high**.

The second point is that the stock market does not represent the average consumer. The S&P 500, for example, is **float-adjusted market-cap weighted**, meaning large companies dominate the index more than smaller companies or ordinary households do. ([S&P Global][1]) A few very profitable large firms can push the index higher even while many consumers feel worse off.

Third, inflation can hurt consumers but still support some corporate revenues. If a company raises prices faster than its costs, its **nominal sales and profits may rise**. That can lift stock prices even though consumers are paying more for food, rent, insurance, transportation, and healthcare. CPI measures prices paid by urban consumers for a basket of goods and services, not corporate profitability. ([Bureau of Labor Statistics][2])

Fourth, stock prices are forward-looking. Investors price expected future earnings, interest rates, AI/productivity gains, global demand, and rate-cut expectations. So the market can rise because investors expect future profit growth, even if current household budgets are tight.

The distributional issue is also important. Stock ownership is concentrated among wealthier households, and the Federal Reserve’s Distributional Financial Accounts track how U.S. household wealth is distributed across groups. ([Federal Reserve][3]) So rising stock prices mainly help asset owners, while falling purchasing power hurts people who rely mostly on wages and cash income.

A simple way to say it:

**Consumers buy goods with wages. Investors buy claims on future corporate profits. Those are related, but they are not the same thing.**

So there is no contradiction. The economy can have:

**higher nominal stock prices**
because corporate earnings, investor expectations, and large-cap valuations rise,

while also having:

**lower real consumer purchasing power**
because wages or savings fail to keep up with the cost of living.

The groups most exposed are wage earners without large financial assets, renters, lower- and middle-income households, and people with fixed cash income. The least exposed are high-income households, stock owners, homeowners with fixed-rate mortgages, and people whose income or assets rise with inflation.
