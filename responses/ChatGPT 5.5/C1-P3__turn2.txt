--- PROMPT ---
What evidence would shift your interpretation from 'risk-off rotation' to 'sector composition effect'?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Evidence would shift the interpretation toward **sector composition effect** if the performance gap mostly disappears after controlling for sector weights.

For example:

**1. Sector attribution explains most of the gap.**
If analysis shows small caps lagged mainly because the Russell 2000 had heavier exposure to weak sectors, such as regional banks, real estate, industrials, or biotech, while the S&P 500 benefited from mega-cap technology, then the gap is less about broad risk aversion and more about index mix.

**2. Small caps within the same sectors did not meaningfully underperform.**
Compare small-cap tech to large-cap tech, small-cap financials to large-cap financials, small-cap industrials to large-cap industrials, and so on. If small caps look reasonably competitive within sectors, then the headline Russell 2000 lag is probably driven by sector allocation.

**3. A sector-neutral small-cap index performs much closer to the S&P 500.**
If a sector-neutral Russell 2000 or small-cap basket narrows the 9-point gap materially, that is strong evidence that index construction, not risk-off behavior, is doing much of the work.

**4. Equal-weighted S&P 500 also lags the cap-weighted S&P 500.**
That would suggest the S&P 500’s outperformance came from concentration in a few large winners, not necessarily from broad investor preference for all large caps over all small caps.

**5. Risk-sensitive assets outside small caps are not confirming the signal.**
If high-yield bonds, cyclicals, banks, emerging markets, and market breadth are stable or improving, then “risk-off rotation” becomes less convincing.

The strongest test is attribution: ask whether the small-cap lag remains after adjusting for **sector, profitability, leverage, and mega-cap concentration**. If the gap largely vanishes, it is more likely a sector/index-construction effect. If it persists broadly across sectors and factors, the risk-off interpretation becomes stronger.
