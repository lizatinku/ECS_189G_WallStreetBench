--- PROMPT ---
U.S. 30-year mortgage rates went from roughly 3% in 2021 to a peak near 8% in late 2023 and now sit at about 6.37%. What does this mean for housing affordability and household demand for homes?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
That swing in mortgage rates is enormous in housing terms—it changes what people can afford far more than most price movements.

A simple way to see it: monthly payments, not home prices, drive affordability for most buyers.

* At **~3% (2021)**: borrowing was historically cheap → buyers could afford *much more house* for the same monthly payment.
* At **~8% (2023 peak)**: monthly payments on the same home jumped **~70–90%** versus 3% rates.
* At **~6.37% (now)**: payments are still **materially higher than 2021**, but noticeably lower than the worst point.

**Bottom line:**

* Affordability **collapsed** from 2021 → 2023
* It has **partially improved**, but is still **far worse than the pandemic-era baseline**

This means many households:

* Qualify for smaller loans
* Need larger down payments
* Or are priced out entirely

Higher rates don’t eliminate demand—they **delay and distort it**.

### What’s happened:

* **Fewer buyers** can afford to enter the market → demand drops
* **First-time buyers** are hit hardest (no equity cushion)
* Many households choose to:

  * Rent longer
  * Buy smaller/cheaper homes
  * Move to lower-cost regions

### But there’s a key nuance:

Demand is **interest-rate sensitive, not permanently destroyed**

* When rates fell from ~8% → ~6.37%, some buyers **re-entered**
* There’s likely **“pent-up demand”** waiting for lower rates

## 3) The “lock-in effect” (a critical second-order impact)

This is where things get more interesting.

Millions of homeowners locked in ~3% mortgages in 2020–2021.
Now:

* Selling their home means giving up a **very cheap loan**
* Buying a new home means taking a **6–7% loan**

So many people simply **don’t move**.

**Result:**

* Housing **supply also shrinks**
* Fewer listings offset weaker demand

This is why:

* Prices didn’t crash as much as expected
* Inventory remains unusually tight

---

## 4) What you *can* conclude vs. what you can’t

### Reasonable conclusions:

* Housing affordability is still **structurally worse than 2021**
* Demand is **below potential**, especially for entry-level buyers
* The market is **rate-driven**—small rate moves matter a lot
* Supply constraints (lock-in effect) are **propping up prices**

### Premature or risky conclusions:

* “Housing demand is weak” → it’s **suppressed, not gone**
* “Prices must fall” → supply constraints complicate that
* “6.37% is normal so things are fine” → affordability depends on **prices + incomes + rates**, not rates alone

---

## 5) Big picture

Think of the housing market like a tug-of-war:

* **Higher rates → ↓ affordability → ↓ demand**
* **But also → ↓ mobility → ↓ supply**

Right now, both sides are pulling down activity, which is why:

* Transaction volume is low
* Prices are relatively sticky
