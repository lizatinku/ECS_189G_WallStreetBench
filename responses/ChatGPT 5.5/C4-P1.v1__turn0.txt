--- PROMPT ---
The dollar has gained noticeably against several major currencies in the past year. Walk through who benefits and who loses across students, multinationals, and trade-exposed firms.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
A stronger dollar means **$1 buys more foreign currency**, but **foreign currencies buy fewer dollars**. So the impact depends on whether someone is paying in dollars, earning in dollars, importing, exporting, or translating foreign income back into dollars. The Fed’s broad dollar index is a trade-weighted measure against major U.S. trading partners, so it is a useful benchmark even though individual currencies can move differently. ([Federal Reserve][1])

### International students

**Losers:** international students in the U.S. whose families earn income in weaker home currencies. Tuition, rent, health insurance, and daily expenses are mostly priced in dollars, so their effective cost rises. For example, if tuition and living costs are **$60,000 per year** and the home currency falls **10%** against the dollar, the family effectively needs about **10% more home currency** to pay the same U.S. bill.

**Beneficiaries:** students from the U.S. studying abroad, or international students whose savings/income are already in dollars. Their dollars go further for rent, food, travel, and tuition abroad.

### Multinational companies

**U.S.-based multinationals lose on foreign revenue translation.** If Apple, Microsoft, or Coca-Cola earns revenue in euros, yen, or pounds, those foreign earnings convert into fewer dollars when reported in U.S. financial statements. Even if unit sales abroad are unchanged, reported dollar revenue can fall.

**They may benefit on imported inputs or overseas investment.** A strong dollar makes foreign labor, components, factories, and acquisitions cheaper in dollar terms. A U.S. firm buying machinery from Japan or services from India may face lower dollar costs.

**Non-U.S. multinationals with dollar debt are hurt.** If a company earns in local currency but borrowed in dollars, its interest and principal payments become more expensive in local-currency terms.

### Import/export businesses

**U.S. importers benefit first.** Retailers, wholesalers, and firms that buy foreign goods can import products more cheaply. This can improve profit margins or lower prices for consumers.

**U.S. exporters are hurt.** American goods become more expensive for foreign buyers. A U.S. machine priced at **$100,000** becomes harder to sell abroad if foreign customers need much more local currency to buy it.

**Foreign exporters to the U.S. can benefit.** If they produce in a weaker currency but sell into the U.S. market, their goods become more price-competitive. They may either cut dollar prices to gain market share or keep prices stable and earn higher local-currency revenue.

### Timing

The group hurt most quickly is usually **international students paying U.S. expenses from foreign-currency income**, because tuition, rent, and living costs are immediate.

The longest-lag effect is often on **multinational companies and exporters**, because contracts, hedging, pricing decisions, and supply-chain adjustments delay the impact. Over time, though, a strong dollar can seriously reduce export competitiveness and reported foreign earnings.
