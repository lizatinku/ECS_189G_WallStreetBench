--- PROMPT ---
The dollar has gained noticeably against several major currencies in the past year. Walk through who benefits and who loses across students, multinationals, and trade-exposed firms.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
I can’t verify the latest “past year” exchange-rate move because web search is disabled here. But assuming the dollar has appreciated noticeably against major currencies, the effects usually look like this:

## Students

U.S. students studying abroad generally benefit. A stronger dollar buys more foreign currency, so tuition, rent, food, travel, and local expenses abroad become cheaper in dollar terms. A semester in Europe, Japan, Canada, or the U.K. becomes more affordable for families whose income or savings are in dollars.

International students in the U.S. usually lose, especially if their families earn in weaker currencies. U.S. tuition, housing, and living costs become more expensive when converted into their home currency. That can force students to draw down more savings, borrow more, work more, or choose cheaper schools.

U.S. students with foreign scholarships or stipends lose if those payments are denominated in a weakening foreign currency. The stipend converts into fewer dollars.

## Multinationals

U.S.-based multinationals often lose on translation. Revenue earned in euros, yen, pounds, yuan, or other currencies converts into fewer dollars on financial statements. Even if foreign sales volumes are unchanged, reported dollar revenue and profit can fall.

They may also lose competitiveness abroad. If their costs are mostly in dollars but they sell overseas, their products become more expensive relative to local competitors unless they cut prices and accept lower margins.

But multinationals with foreign costs can benefit. A U.S. company that pays workers, suppliers, rent, or utilities abroad in weaker local currencies may see those costs fall in dollar terms. Firms with global supply chains may get some margin relief.

Foreign multinationals selling into the U.S. often benefit. Their U.S. sales translate into more home currency, and the strong dollar can make the American market especially attractive.

## Trade-exposed firms

U.S. importers generally benefit. A stronger dollar makes foreign-made goods, components, machinery, raw materials, and finished products cheaper in dollar terms. Retailers, manufacturers using imported inputs, and firms buying foreign equipment may see lower costs.

U.S. exporters generally lose. Their goods become more expensive for foreign buyers, which can reduce demand. They may need to discount prices, absorb margin pressure, or lose market share to foreign rivals.

Domestic firms competing with imports can lose. If foreign products become cheaper because of the stronger dollar, U.S. producers face tougher price competition at home. This matters for industries like manufacturing, agriculture, steel, autos, machinery, apparel, and some chemicals.

Firms with dollar-denominated debt outside the U.S. can lose badly. A company earning revenue in a weaker local currency but owing debt in dollars has to spend more local currency to service the same dollar debt.

Commodity-linked firms face mixed effects. Since many commodities are priced in dollars, a stronger dollar can pressure global commodity demand and prices. That can hurt exporters of oil, metals, and agricultural products, though firms that import commodities may benefit from cheaper inputs.

## The clean summary

A stronger dollar tends to help Americans who buy foreign goods, travel abroad, study abroad, or import inputs. It tends to hurt U.S. exporters, foreign students in the U.S., U.S. firms with overseas earnings, and foreign borrowers with dollar debt.

The biggest winners are dollar earners spending abroad or buying imports. The biggest losers are non-dollar earners who must pay dollar costs, and firms whose foreign revenues shrink when translated back into dollars.
