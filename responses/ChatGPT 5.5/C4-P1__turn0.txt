--- PROMPT ---
The U.S. dollar has strengthened significantly against several international currencies over the past year. How could this affect international students, multinational companies, and import/export businesses? Discuss both positive and negative economic impacts.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---

## International students

A stronger dollar makes the U.S. more expensive for students whose families earn income in weaker foreign currencies.

Negative impacts include higher effective tuition, rent, food, and living costs. For example, a student paying $50,000 per year in U.S. expenses needs more of their home currency to cover the same dollar bill. This can increase financial stress, force students to work more, reduce discretionary spending, or discourage future enrollment in U.S. universities.

Positive impacts are more limited. Students who already hold savings in dollars, receive dollar-denominated scholarships, or earn income in the U.S. may be less affected. Also, if they travel outside the U.S., their dollars may go further abroad.

## Multinational companies

For U.S.-based multinationals, a strong dollar can hurt reported profits from overseas operations. Revenue earned in euros, yen, yuan, pounds, or other currencies converts into fewer dollars, lowering dollar-denominated sales and earnings. It can also make U.S.-made goods more expensive abroad, reducing export competitiveness.

However, there are benefits. U.S. firms that buy foreign inputs, components, or services may see lower costs. Overseas acquisitions can become cheaper in dollar terms. Companies with large import needs or global supply chains may benefit from improved purchasing power.

For foreign multinationals operating in the U.S., the effect can be positive if they earn substantial dollar revenue, because those dollars translate into more home currency. But their U.S. operations may become more expensive to maintain if they need to send workers, capital, or supplies into the U.S.

## Import and export businesses

Importers generally benefit from a stronger dollar. Foreign goods become cheaper in dollar terms, which can lower costs for retailers, manufacturers, and consumers. This can help reduce inflationary pressure, especially for imported goods such as electronics, machinery, clothing, and commodities priced internationally.

Exporters often suffer. U.S. products become more expensive for foreign buyers, which can reduce demand. Farmers, manufacturers, tourism operators, universities, and service exporters may all feel pressure if foreign customers find U.S. prices less attractive.

## Broader economic impacts

A stronger dollar can help consumers by making imports cheaper and foreign travel more affordable. It can also reduce inflation by lowering the cost of imported goods and raw materials.

But it can widen the trade deficit if imports rise and exports fall. It may hurt U.S. manufacturing, agriculture, and tourism. It can also create stress in emerging markets, especially for countries or companies with dollar-denominated debt, because they need more local currency to repay the same dollar loans.

Overall, a stronger dollar benefits U.S. consumers, import-heavy businesses, and dollar earners, but it hurts foreign students paying U.S. costs, U.S. exporters, and companies that rely heavily on foreign-currency revenues.
