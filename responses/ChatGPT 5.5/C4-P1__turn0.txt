--- PROMPT ---
The U.S. dollar has strengthened significantly against several international currencies over the past year. How could this affect international students, multinational companies, and import/export businesses? Discuss both positive and negative economic impacts.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Assuming the dollar is stronger relative to the currencies students and firms use, the key idea is: **one U.S. dollar buys more foreign currency, but one unit of foreign currency buys fewer U.S. dollars.** The Federal Reserve’s broad dollar index is a trade-weighted measure of the dollar against major U.S. trading partners, and higher index levels indicate dollar appreciation. Recent H.10 data put the broad dollar index around 119 in late May 2026. ([Federal Reserve][1])

| Group                                  | Positive impacts                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              | Negative impacts                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **International students in the U.S.** | Students whose families earn or hold **U.S. dollars** benefit: tuition, rent, travel, and daily expenses abroad become cheaper in foreign-currency terms. U.S.-based scholarships, assistantships, or wages also have higher purchasing power when sent home. | Students funded by families earning in weaker currencies suffer: U.S. tuition, rent, insurance, and living costs become more expensive after conversion. This can increase financial stress, reduce consumption, or force students to work more hours.                                                                                                                                                               |
| **Multinational companies**            | U.S. multinationals that import inputs, materials, or foreign services may see lower dollar costs. A strong dollar also makes foreign assets, acquisitions, and overseas investment cheaper for U.S. firms.                                                   | U.S. companies earning revenue abroad face a **translation loss**: foreign sales convert into fewer dollars. Their exports also become more expensive to foreign buyers, reducing competitiveness. Reuters notes that when the dollar strengthens, the opposite of a weak-dollar export boost occurs: foreign revenues and international investments are worth less when converted back into dollars. ([Reuters][2]) |
| **Import businesses**                  | U.S. importers benefit because foreign goods become cheaper in dollar terms. This can lower costs for retailers and manufacturers using imported inputs, and may reduce inflationary pressure for U.S. consumers.                                             | Foreign producers selling into the U.S. may receive less in their own currency or face pressure to cut prices. Domestic U.S. producers competing with imports may lose market share because imported goods become relatively cheaper.                                                                                                                                                                                |
| **Export businesses**                  | Foreign exporters selling to the U.S. may gain because American consumers can buy more foreign goods with stronger dollars. Export-led economies may benefit if their goods become cheaper for U.S. buyers.                                                   | U.S. exporters are hurt because American goods become more expensive abroad. This can reduce export volume, revenue, and employment in export-heavy industries such as manufacturing, agriculture, tourism, and education services.                                                                                                                                                                                  |

A stronger dollar can also create broader global pressure. Many international contracts, commodities, and debts are priced in dollars. The IMF finds that dollar appreciation can reduce trade volumes and tighten financial conditions, especially in emerging markets; it notes that imports can decline more sharply than exports and that credit availability and capital inflows often worsen. ([IMF][3]) The World Bank similarly explains that countries or firms with dollar-denominated debt face higher repayment burdens when their local currency weakens, while dollar-priced imports become more expensive in domestic-currency terms. ([World Bank Blogs][4])

So the overall effect is **mixed**: the strong dollar helps U.S. consumers, importers, and dollar-funded students, but hurts U.S. exporters, foreign-currency-funded students, and firms or countries with dollar debt. Economically, it can lower U.S. import prices but weaken U.S. export competitiveness and increase financial stress abroad.
