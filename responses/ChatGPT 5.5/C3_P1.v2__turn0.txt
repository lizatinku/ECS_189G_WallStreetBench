--- PROMPT ---
Why do companies sometimes see their stock prices go up on layoff announcements? Use the recent Coinbase / Oracle / Atlassian examples.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
When investors reward a company after layoffs, they are usually not “celebrating unemployment.” They are reacting to what the layoffs imply about future profits, cash flow, or management strategy.

With companies like Coinbase, Oracle, and Atlassian, the market’s logic has broadly been:

1. costs down now
2. expected productivity up later
3. more capital redirected toward AI growth

That combination can improve expected earnings per share — at least on paper.

For example, Coinbase announced it would cut about 14% of staff while reorganizing around AI-driven workflows and flatter management structures. ([TechCrunch][1]) Investors often interpret that as management saying: “We think we can generate similar or greater output with fewer employees.” If Wall Street believes that claim, future margins may look better.

Oracle’s case is slightly different. Investors appear to view its layoffs as part of a broader capital reallocation toward AI infrastructure and cloud growth. Analysts have argued that Oracle’s aggressive AI investments could position it well in enterprise AI, even though the spending is enormous. ([Investors.com][2]) So the stock reaction can become:

* negative for employees in the short run,
* but positive for shareholders if investors expect higher long-term returns on that AI spending.

Atlassian explicitly framed its cuts as a way to free resources for AI and enterprise sales. ([TechCrunch][3]) Markets often reward this because software investors currently place a premium on companies seen as “AI leaders” rather than firms merely maintaining headcount.

There’s also a more mechanical financial reason. Labor is one of the largest operating expenses for tech companies. If a company cuts 10% of staff, analysts may immediately revise profit forecasts upward. Even before any AI benefit materializes, lower payroll alone can improve projected operating margins.

A simplified example:

* Revenue forecast unchanged: $10B
* Expenses fall by $500M
* Expected profit rises materially

Since stock prices reflect expectations about future profits, shares can rise even if the broader human impact is severe.

But there are important caveats.

A stock pop after layoffs does *not* prove the layoffs were wise. Markets are making probabilistic bets under uncertainty. Sometimes layoffs genuinely improve efficiency; other times they damage morale, slow innovation, weaken customer service, or signal underlying weakness.

That’s why similar announcements can produce different reactions:

* “We’re cutting costs because demand collapsed” often hurts a stock.
* “We’re restructuring to redirect resources into high-growth AI businesses” may help a stock.

The narrative matters.

There’s also a speculative element in today’s AI cycle. Investors are currently rewarding firms that appear disciplined about costs *and* aggressive about AI positioning. Some critics argue companies are invoking “AI transformation” partly because markets respond favorably to it. ([Reddit][4])

So the market reaction is not a moral judgment. It’s a forward-looking estimate — sometimes accurate, sometimes wildly optimistic — about whether the company will produce more cash flow per share in the future.
