=== Prompt ID: C2-P1  |  Category: C2  |  Turn: 1 ===

--- PROMPT ---
Walk through a concrete example: a household earning $90,000/year considering a $450,000 home. What changes between a 3% and a 6.37% mortgage?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Assume:

* Household income: **$90,000/year**, or **$7,500/month gross**
* Home price: **$450,000**
* Down payment: **20%**, or **$90,000**
* Mortgage amount: **$360,000**
* Loan: **30-year fixed**
* Property tax estimate: **1.2% of home value**, or **$450/month**
* Home insurance estimate: **$150/month**
* No PMI because of 20% down

### Monthly payment comparison

| Item                           |      3% mortgage |   6.37% mortgage |
| ------------------------------ | ---------------: | ---------------: |
| Principal & interest           | about **$1,518** | about **$2,244** |
| Property taxes                 |         **$450** |         **$450** |
| Insurance                      |         **$150** |         **$150** |
| Estimated monthly housing cost |       **$2,118** |       **$2,844** |

The higher rate adds about **$726/month**, or about **$8,700/year**, for the same home at the same price.

### Affordability impact

At $90,000/year, gross monthly income is **$7,500**.

| Measure                   |      3% mortgage |   6.37% mortgage |
| ------------------------- | ---------------: | ---------------: |
| Estimated housing payment | **$2,118/month** | **$2,844/month** |
| Share of gross income     |        **28.2%** |        **37.9%** |

At **3%**, this home is near a common affordability threshold: around 28% of gross income.

At **6.37%**, the same home becomes much more stretched. A housing-cost ratio near 38% may still be possible for some borrowers, but it leaves less room for student loans, car payments, childcare, repairs, savings, and emergencies. Many lenders would scrutinize the full debt-to-income ratio closely.

### Buying power falls sharply

To keep the same principal-and-interest payment as the 3% mortgage, the household could borrow only about **$243,000** at 6.37%, instead of **$360,000**.

With a 20% down payment, that implies a home price around:

**$243,000 ÷ 80% = about $304,000**

So, purely from the rate change, the household’s buying power falls from a **$450,000 home** to roughly a **$304,000 home** for the same mortgage payment. That is a drop of about **32%** in affordable home price, assuming the same down payment percentage.

### What changes in practical terms

At **3%**, the household can plausibly consider the $450,000 home, assuming modest other debts.

At **6.37%**, they may need to do one or more of the following:

* Buy a cheaper home
* Make a larger down payment
* Accept a much tighter monthly budget
* Wait for rates or prices to fall
* Look for seller concessions or a mortgage-rate buydown
* Choose a smaller home or different location

The uncertainty is that home prices, taxes, insurance, wages, and inventory all matter too. If prices fall, affordability improves. If insurance or taxes rise, affordability worsens. And if supply remains tight, prices may not fall enough to offset the higher mortgage rate.
