=== Prompt ID: C2-P3  |  Category: C2  |  Turn: 0 ===

--- PROMPT ---
A graduate student is deciding between (i) renting at $1,800/month in a city with 4% annual rent growth or (ii) buying a $320,000 condo at a 6.5% mortgage with 10% down. Walk through the key factors that would tip the decision one way or the other, and where uncertainty matters most.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The given numbers are deceptively close: the condo’s **mortgage principal and interest is about $1,820/month**, almost identical to the starting rent of **$1,800/month**.

But that does **not** mean buying is automatically equal. The decision turns on the costs and risks around that payment.

Assuming a 30-year mortgage:

| Item                                 |                  Renting |                                           Buying |
| ------------------------------------ | -----------------------: | -----------------------------------------------: |
| Starting monthly payment             |                   $1,800 |                            ~$1,820 mortgage only |
| Five-year average rent, at 4% growth |            ~$1,950/month | Mortgage fixed, but taxes/HOA/insurance may rise |
| Upfront cash                         |   Usually deposit + fees |                    ~$32,000 down + closing costs |
| Flexibility                          |                     High |                           Low, due to sale costs |
| Wealth-building                      | Invest savings elsewhere |                   Equity + possible appreciation |

The key point: **the buyer’s true monthly cost is not $1,820.** Add property tax, HOA dues, insurance, maintenance, PMI if applicable, and possible special assessments. A realistic all-in condo cost might easily be **$2,400–$3,000/month**, depending heavily on the city and building.

### What would tip the decision toward renting

Renting looks better if the student may move within the next few years. Buying has large transaction costs: closing costs when buying, and broker/selling costs when selling. If they sell after only 2–4 years, even modest appreciation may not be enough to overcome those costs.

Renting also looks better if the condo has high HOA fees, weak reserves, rising insurance costs, or risk of special assessments. Condo ownership can be especially sensitive to building-level problems that renters do not directly bear.

Renting also wins if the student can invest the down payment elsewhere. The buyer needs roughly **$32,000 down**, plus closing costs. That cash has an opportunity cost. If the student is financially stretched, keeping liquidity may be more valuable than building home equity.

### What would tip the decision toward buying

Buying looks better if the student expects to stay for a long time, likely **5–7+ years**. The longer they stay, the more the fixed mortgage payment becomes attractive relative to rent that compounds at 4% annually.

Buying also improves if the condo appreciates meaningfully. For example, at 3% annual appreciation, a $320,000 condo would be worth about **$371,000 after five years**. That appreciation could outweigh many ownership costs. But if prices are flat or fall, the case weakens quickly.

Buying is also more attractive if the HOA is reasonable, the building has healthy reserves, property taxes are manageable, and the student values stability, control, and protection from future rent increases.

### Where uncertainty matters most

The biggest uncertainty is **how long the student will stay**. A short holding period strongly favors renting; a long one gives buying time to work.

The second-biggest uncertainty is **home price appreciation**. A small difference between 0%, 2%, and 4% annual appreciation can completely change the result.

The third is **condo-specific cost risk**: HOA increases, special assessments, insurance increases, major repairs, and resale liquidity. These can matter more than the mortgage rate.

Rent growth matters too, but less than people often think. At 4% annual growth, rent rises from **$1,800 today to about $2,106 in year five** and about **$2,562 in year ten**. That helps the buying case over time, but only if ownership costs are controlled.

### Bottom line

At these numbers, **renting probably looks better for a graduate student who needs flexibility, may move after graduation, or would be cash-constrained after the down payment.**

Buying becomes compelling only if they are confident they will stay long enough, the condo’s HOA and building finances are strong, and they are comfortable taking on appreciation risk and repair/assessment risk.

The decision is less about the $1,800 rent versus the $1,820 mortgage and more about this question:

**Is the student buying a stable long-term housing asset, or taking on a leveraged, illiquid investment during a life stage where flexibility may be unusually valuable?**
