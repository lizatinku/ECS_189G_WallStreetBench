--- PROMPT ---
A graduate student is deciding between (i) renting at $1,800/month in a city with 4% annual rent growth or (ii) buying a $320,000 condo at a 6.5% mortgage with 10% down. Walk through the key factors that would tip the decision one way or the other, and where uncertainty matters most.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Assuming a **30-year fixed mortgage**, the condo purchase looks deceptively close to renting at first:

* Condo price: **$320,000**
* Down payment: **10% = $32,000**
* Loan: **$288,000**
* Mortgage rate: **6.5%**
* Estimated principal + interest: **about $1,820/month**
* Current rent: **$1,800/month**, rising to **$1,872/month in year 2** and about **$2,190/month after 5 annual 4% increases**

So the mortgage payment alone is almost equal to rent. But the real decision depends on everything around that number.

## What would tip toward renting

Renting is better if the student may move after graduation, wants flexibility, or is unsure about income/location. Buying has upfront closing costs, and the CFPB notes that buyers generally pay transaction costs at closing, while ongoing ownership costs can include property taxes and homeowners insurance through escrow. ([Consumer Financial Protection Bureau][1])

Renting also wins if the condo has high HOA fees, possible special assessments, weak resale demand, or little expected appreciation. With only 10% down, a modest price decline plus selling costs could wipe out much of the student’s equity.

Also, because the down payment is below 20%, the buyer would likely need mortgage insurance/PMI, which adds to monthly cost. ([Consumer Financial Protection Bureau][2])

## What would tip toward buying

Buying becomes more attractive if the student expects to stay for several years, rent keeps rising at 4% or more, the condo appreciates, and HOA/tax/insurance costs are manageable. The mortgage payment is mostly fixed, while rent compounds upward.

There is also forced saving: in year 1, about **$3,219** of mortgage payments would go to principal, while about **$18,625** would be interest. So early on, most of the payment is still a financing cost, but some equity is being built.

Buying also becomes stronger if the student can later refinance at a lower rate, rent out the condo after moving, or has stable family/financial support that reduces liquidity risk.

## The biggest uncertainty

The **holding period** matters most. If they stay only 1–3 years, renting probably wins because buying and selling costs are too large relative to the time owned. If they stay 5–7+ years, buying has more time to benefit from rent inflation, equity buildup, and possible appreciation.

The second-biggest uncertainty is the **true all-in monthly ownership cost**. The mortgage is about **$1,820**, but after property tax, insurance, HOA, PMI, maintenance, and possible repairs, the real monthly cost could easily be much higher than rent.

A rough ownership cash-flow example:

| Item                          |          Estimated monthly cost |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------: |
| Mortgage principal + interest |                          $1,820 |
| Property tax                  |                 maybe $250–$400 |
| HOA                           |                maybe $200–$600+ |
| Insurance                     |                  maybe $50–$150 |
| PMI                           |                 maybe $100–$250 |
| Maintenance/reserve           |                 maybe $150–$300 |
| **Total**                     | **roughly $2,570–$3,520/month** |

So the core question is not “$1,800 rent vs. $1,820 mortgage.” It is closer to:

**$1,800 rent with flexibility**
vs.
**$2,600–$3,500 ownership cost with equity upside but much higher risk.**

## Bottom line

For a graduate student, I would lean **rent** unless they are confident they will stay in the city for at least 5 years, have emergency savings beyond the down payment, and the condo has low HOA/assessment risk.

Buying starts to make sense if the condo is unusually good value, rent growth is truly persistent, the student wants to remain in the area long-term, and they can handle negative surprises without being forced to sell.
