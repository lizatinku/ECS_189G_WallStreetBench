--- PROMPT ---
Is the market's positive reaction to layoffs evidence that layoffs are 'good' for the company long term? Why or why not?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Not by itself. A **positive stock reaction is evidence that investors expect layoffs to improve near-term financial performance**, but it is **not proof that layoffs are good for the company long term**.

The market may reward layoffs because investors immediately update expectations about:

**1. Lower costs and higher margins**
If a company reduces payroll while keeping revenue stable, expected profit margins and earnings per share can rise. That is especially attractive when investors believe the company had over-hired or can use AI/tools to maintain output with fewer employees.

**2. Management discipline**
Layoffs can signal that management is willing to control expenses, redirect resources, and prioritize higher-return projects. In AI-related cases, investors may interpret cuts as “we are moving capital from lower-productivity labor to higher-growth AI investment.”

**3. Short-term EPS math**
Even if total revenue does not grow much, cutting costs can mechanically improve earnings per share. Stock prices often react strongly to expected EPS and free cash flow.

But that is still only a **short-term market interpretation**, not a long-term verdict. Academic evidence is mixed and often negative: one study found the average two-day abnormal return around layoff announcements was about **−1.2%**, especially negative when layoffs were driven by declining demand. A later meta-analysis of more than **34,000 layoff announcements** also found that the average investor reaction was significantly negative, even though some individual cases are positive. ([ScienceDirect][1])

The long-term outcome depends on **why** the company is cutting and **what happens after**:

If layoffs remove genuine excess headcount, simplify bureaucracy, and free money for productive AI/cloud/product investment, they can help long-term profitability.

But if layoffs cut important engineers, sales staff, customer support, or research capacity, they can weaken innovation, hurt morale, slow execution, and reduce future growth. In that case, the stock may rise initially but underperform later.

So the key distinction is:

**A stock-price increase means investors think layoffs may improve future cash flows. It does not prove the layoffs will actually create long-term value.**

For Coinbase, Oracle, and Atlassian, the positive interpretation is “leaner company + AI investment + better margins.” The riskier interpretation is “growth is harder, AI spending is expensive, and the company is cutting people because pressure is rising.” The market’s first reaction reflects expectations; long-term performance depends on whether those expectations come true.
