=== Prompt ID: C3-P3  |  Category: C3  |  Turn: 2 ===

--- PROMPT ---
How should an investor interpret the 22% jump in the target — is it 'free money' for someone who already owned shares?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
For someone who already owned the target before the announcement, the 22% jump is a real mark-to-market gain, but it is not “free money” in the sense of riskless value.

It mostly represents the market rapidly repricing the target from a standalone company to a takeover candidate. The new price usually reflects:

**Offer value minus deal risk.** If the buyer offers, say, $50 per share and the stock trades at $47 after the announcement, the $3 gap is the market’s estimate of risks such as regulatory approval, shareholder approval, financing, timing, or renegotiation.

**Probability-weighted outcomes.** The post-announcement price blends scenarios: the deal closes at the offer price, the deal breaks and the stock falls back toward its unaffected price, another bidder appears, or terms change.

**Time value.** Even if the deal is likely to close, shareholders may wait months to receive cash or buyer stock. The spread compensates investors for that waiting period and uncertainty.

So for an existing holder, the jump is a windfall from having owned an asset that became more valuable because control of the company is now being sold. But after the announcement, the remaining upside is usually not free money; it is merger-arbitrage risk.

A simple interpretation:

Before announcement: you owned business risk.

After announcement: you own deal-closing risk.

The gain is “earned” by having borne the pre-announcement uncertainty. The remaining spread exists because the market is not treating the deal as guaranteed.
