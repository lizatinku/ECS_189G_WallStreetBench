=== Prompt ID: C3-P3  |  Category: C3  |  Turn: 2 ===

--- PROMPT ---
How should an investor interpret the 22% jump in the target — is it 'free money' for someone who already owned shares?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Not exactly “free money.” It is better interpreted as a **sudden repricing of the target’s shares based on the expected takeover value**.

For someone who already owned the target before the announcement, the 22% jump is a real mark-to-market gain. They benefited because the market now expects the acquirer to pay a premium above the target’s previous standalone value.

But it is not risk-free because the stock price after the announcement reflects probabilities. The target’s shares may trade:

**below the offer price** if investors think there is deal risk, such as regulatory rejection, shareholder opposition, financing problems, or a failed closing.

**near the offer price** if the market thinks the deal is very likely to close.

**above the offer price** if investors think a higher competing bid may appear.

So for an existing shareholder, the gain is real, but it is not “free money” in the sense of guaranteed profit. It is compensation for having already held a risky asset before the positive acquisition news arrived.

For a new investor buying after the 22% jump, the situation is different. They are not capturing the original takeover premium; they are taking a merger-arbitrage bet on whether the deal closes, whether the final price changes, and how long the capital is tied up.
