=== Prompt ID: C2-P2  |  Category: C2  |  Turn: 0 ===

--- PROMPT ---
The Federal Reserve has cut its policy rate by 75 basis points over the past nine months. How might this transmit to (a) credit card APRs, (b) auto loan rates, and (c) the price of long-duration tech stocks? Explain which links are direct and which are indirect.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Taking the 75 bp cut as given, the transmission differs a lot by asset/loan type.

### (a) Credit card APRs: most direct, but not always full

Credit card APRs are often variable and commonly priced as:

**APR = prime rate + issuer margin**

The prime rate tends to move closely with the Fed’s policy rate. So a 75 bp Fed cut can pass through fairly directly into variable credit card APRs, often with a short lag.

But the pass-through may not be exactly 75 bp because card issuers can adjust margins, risk pricing, fees, or promotional terms. Also, credit card APRs are very high relative to the Fed funds rate, so a 75 bp cut may look small: for example, a 24.99% APR might become roughly 24.24%, not dramatically cheaper.

**Link:** direct through prime-rate-linked variable pricing.

---

### (b) Auto loan rates: indirect and less one-for-one

Most auto loans are fixed-rate installment loans. Their rates are influenced by the Fed, but not mechanically tied to the policy rate.

The Fed cut can lower auto rates through several channels:

**Fed cuts → lower short-term rates / lower Treasury yields → cheaper funding for lenders → lower offered auto loan rates**

But auto loan rates also depend on:

borrower credit risk, used-car collateral values, loan term, bank/dealer competition, securitization markets, and credit spreads.

So a 75 bp policy-rate cut might reduce auto loan rates, but the move may be smaller, delayed, or offset if lenders become more worried about defaults.

**Link:** mostly indirect through funding costs, Treasury yields, and credit conditions.

---

### (c) Long-duration tech stocks: indirect, but potentially powerful

Long-duration tech stocks get much of their value from expected cash flows far in the future. In valuation terms:

**Lower discount rates → higher present value of future earnings → higher stock prices**

So Fed cuts can support long-duration tech stocks by reducing risk-free rates and possibly lowering companies’ cost of capital. This effect is especially strong for growth companies whose profits are expected far out in time.

But the link is not mechanical. Stock prices also depend on earnings expectations, AI/product cycles, risk appetite, equity risk premia, and why the Fed is cutting. If cuts happen because inflation is falling and growth remains solid, tech valuations may rise. If cuts happen because recession risk is rising, lower discount rates may be offset by weaker expected earnings or higher risk premia.

**Link:** indirect through discount rates, long-term yields, liquidity, and investor risk appetite.

---

### Bottom line

Credit card APRs have the most direct transmission because many are explicitly tied to prime rates. Auto loan rates are affected indirectly through funding markets and credit conditions. Long-duration tech stocks are also affected indirectly, but the valuation impact can be large because their cash flows are far in the future and therefore highly sensitive to discount-rate changes.
