=== Prompt ID: C2-P2  |  Category: C2  |  Turn: 0 ===

--- PROMPT ---
The Federal Reserve has cut its policy rate by 75 basis points over the past nine months. How might this transmit to (a) credit card APRs, (b) auto loan rates, and (c) the price of long-duration tech stocks? Explain which links are direct and which are indirect.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
A **75 bp Fed cut does not pass through equally** to all rates or asset prices. The Fed directly controls the **overnight policy-rate target**, and that normally affects other short rates and broader financial conditions, but the further you move from overnight lending, the more indirect the link becomes. ([Federal Reserve][1])

### (a) Credit card APRs — **most direct**

This is the clearest pass-through. Most credit cards have **variable APRs** tied to the **prime rate**, and the prime rate closely follows the federal funds rate. The Boston Fed describes the usual formula as:

> credit card APR ≈ prime rate + issuer/customer margin

and notes that prime is typically the federal funds rate plus about 3 percentage points. ([Federal Reserve Bank of Boston][2])

So, if the Fed cuts 75 bp and prime falls roughly 75 bp, a variable credit card APR may also fall about **75 bp**, often within one or two billing cycles. But the borrower-specific margin stays high, so a card at 24.99% might become around 24.24%, not suddenly “cheap.” Current H.15 data show the bank prime loan rate at **6.75%**, consistent with a fed funds target upper limit of **3.75%**. ([Federal Reserve][3])

**Link type:** Direct from Fed funds → prime → variable APR.
**Caveat:** Only matters if the borrower carries a balance.

### (b) Auto loan rates — **indirect and incomplete**

Auto loan rates are not mechanically tied to the Fed funds rate. New auto loans are usually priced from a mix of:

borrower credit score, term length, dealer/manufacturer incentives, bank/ABS funding costs, expected defaults, used-car collateral values, and Treasury/swap yields around the loan’s maturity.

So a Fed cut can help by lowering banks’ funding costs and short-term rates, but the pass-through is weaker. If longer-term Treasury yields stay high, credit spreads widen, car prices rise, or incentives fall, auto loan rates may not fall much. Cox Automotive even notes that new-vehicle loan rates can rise while the Fed is cutting if vehicle supply, incentives, and pricing conditions move the other way. ([Cox Automotive Inc.][4])

Existing fixed-rate auto loans usually **do not change** unless refinanced.

**Link type:** Mostly indirect: Fed funds → funding/yield curve/credit conditions → lender pricing → auto loan rates.
**Caveat:** Credit score can dominate the Fed effect; Cox notes one credit-tier improvement can reduce rates by 2+ percentage points, much larger than a 75 bp Fed move. ([Cox Automotive Inc.][4])

### (c) Long-duration tech stocks — **indirect, but potentially powerful**

The Fed does **not** directly set tech stock prices. But long-duration tech stocks are very sensitive to discount rates because much of their value comes from expected cash flows far in the future.

In simple valuation terms:

[
P = \sum_t \frac{CF_t}{(1+r)^t}
]

When the discount rate (r) falls, far-future cash flows rise more in present value. That is why growth/tech stocks often benefit when markets believe rates will be lower for longer. The Chicago Fed describes three channels from easier monetary policy to stock prices: higher expected profits, lower risk-free discount rates, and lower risk premiums. ([Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago][5])

But this is not automatic. If the Fed cuts because the economy is weakening, investors may lower expected earnings or demand a higher risk premium. Also, tech valuations depend more on **long-term real rates** and risk appetite than on the overnight Fed rate alone. A 75 bp cut in the policy rate does not guarantee a 75 bp fall in the 10-year yield; recent H.15 data show short rates near the policy range while the 10-year Treasury yield was around **4.50%**, illustrating that the long end can move differently. ([Federal Reserve][3])

**Link type:**
Direct valuation channel: lower expected discount rates → higher present value of future earnings.
Indirect channels: Fed cuts → growth expectations, risk appetite, credit conditions, dollar, AI/earnings expectations → tech stock prices.

### Bottom line

Credit cards have the **most direct** transmission. Auto loans have a **partial and indirect** transmission. Long-duration tech stocks can react strongly, but mostly through **market expectations about discount rates, growth, and risk premiums**, not through a mechanical Fed-rate formula.
