--- PROMPT ---
What would be a leading indicator that the oil shock is starting to flow through to consumer prices?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
A strong leading indicator is **retail gasoline prices**, especially the national average price at the pump.

Gasoline flows into consumer prices quickly because it is directly included in CPI and consumers buy it frequently. When crude oil spikes, wholesale gasoline prices usually move first, then retail pump prices follow within **days to a few weeks**. That makes gasoline one of the earliest visible signs that an oil shock is reaching households.

For broader pass-through beyond the gas pump, watch **diesel prices and freight fuel surcharges**. Diesel affects trucking, delivery, agriculture, and distribution costs. If diesel rises and surcharges start showing up in shipping contracts or delivery costs, that suggests the shock may soon appear in prices for groceries, packaged goods, building materials, and retail items.

A practical sequence is:

**Crude oil rises → wholesale gasoline/diesel rises → pump prices and diesel surcharges rise → transportation and input costs rise → consumer goods prices rise.**

Typical timing:

**Gasoline CPI impact:** days to weeks.
**Freight and delivery cost impact:** weeks to a few months.
**Broader goods and food price pass-through:** one to three quarters, depending on contracts, inventories, and pricing power.

So the cleanest early signal is **pump gasoline prices**; the better signal for broader inflation pressure is **diesel plus freight surcharges**.
