=== Prompt ID: C4-P3  |  Category: C4  |  Turn: 2 ===

--- PROMPT ---
What would be a leading indicator that the oil shock is starting to flow through to consumer prices?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
A good **leading indicator** is **retail gasoline prices**, especially the weekly movement in regular gasoline prices.

Oil shocks usually reach consumers first through:

**crude oil → wholesale gasoline / diesel → retail gasoline prices → transport costs → broader CPI**

So the earliest signs are:

| Indicator                                     | Why it matters                                                                | Timing                                    |
| --------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| **Retail gasoline prices**                    | Directly enters consumer inflation and is highly visible to households.       | **Days to weeks** after oil move          |
| **RBOB gasoline futures**                     | Market expectation for future gasoline prices before they appear at the pump. | **Immediate to days**                     |
| **Diesel prices**                             | Important for trucking, logistics, agriculture, and delivery costs.           | **Days to weeks**                         |
| **Airfares / freight rates**                  | Shows whether fuel costs are being passed through by transportation firms.    | **Weeks to 1–2 months**                   |
| **PPI: transportation, chemicals, packaging** | Shows input-cost pressure before it reaches final consumer goods.             | **1–3 months**                            |
| **CPI gasoline and energy services**          | Confirms the pass-through has reached consumers.                              | **1 month+**, depending on release timing |

The **best early signal** is probably:

> **Rising gasoline and diesel prices, especially if they persist for several weeks.**

If gasoline rises only briefly, the inflation effect may stay concentrated in the energy component. But if **diesel, freight, packaging, and producer prices** also rise, that suggests the oil shock is moving beyond the gas pump into broader consumer prices.

A clean way to say it:

**Watch gasoline for the first-round effect; watch diesel, freight rates, and PPI for evidence of broader pass-through.**
