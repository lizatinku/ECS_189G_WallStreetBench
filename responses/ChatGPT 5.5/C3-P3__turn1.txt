--- PROMPT ---
What would have to be true for the acquirer's stock to rise on the announcement instead?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
For the acquirer’s stock to rise, the market would need to believe the deal is **value-creating for the buyer’s shareholders**, not just good for the target.

That usually means several things would have to be true:

1. **The price is seen as attractive**

   The market believes the acquirer is not overpaying. Even with a premium, the target may be undervalued, strategically important, or worth more inside the acquirer than as a standalone company.

2. **Synergies are credible and large**

   Investors believe the combined airline can generate real benefits, such as lower costs, better route coverage, higher load factors, stronger pricing power, improved aircraft utilization, or shared loyalty-program value. The key is that these synergies must exceed the acquisition premium.

3. **Integration risk is manageable**

   The market trusts that the acquirer can integrate fleets, labor contracts, operations, IT systems, and regional routes without major disruption.

4. **The deal improves the acquirer’s strategic position**

   For example, the regional carrier may give the acquirer access to valuable airport slots, feeder routes, underserved markets, or a stronger regional network that supports its mainline business.

5. **Financing is not too costly**

   If the acquirer can fund the deal without taking on excessive debt, issuing undervalued stock, or weakening its balance sheet, investors may respond more positively.

6. **Management has credibility**

   If the acquirer has a strong track record of disciplined capital allocation and successful integrations, the market is more likely to believe the projected benefits.

So the acquirer’s stock rises when investors think:

**Value of target inside acquirer + expected synergies − purchase price − integration/financing risk > 0**

In plain English: the market believes the buyer is getting more than it is paying for.
