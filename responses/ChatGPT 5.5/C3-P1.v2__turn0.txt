--- PROMPT ---
Why do companies sometimes see their stock prices go up on layoff announcements? Use the recent Coinbase / Oracle / Atlassian examples.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Companies’ stocks can rise on layoff announcements because the market often treats layoffs less as “bad news for employees” and more as “management is protecting margins.” The reaction is usually about expected future cash flow, not whether the announcement is socially positive.

Using Coinbase, Oracle, and Atlassian as examples, the logic is roughly this:

**1. Layoffs can signal lower costs and higher future earnings.**
Employees are a major expense, especially at software and tech companies. When Coinbase cuts headcount after a crypto downturn, or Atlassian trims teams after a hiring boom, investors may quickly model lower operating expenses. If revenue does not fall by the same amount, expected profit margins improve, so the stock can rise.

**2. The market may see the company as becoming more disciplined.**
Tech firms often overhire during boom periods. A layoff announcement can be interpreted as management admitting that growth assumptions were too optimistic and now correcting course. In Coinbase’s case, layoffs during crypto weakness can be read as survival-and-profitability discipline. In Atlassian’s case, cuts can be framed as reallocating resources toward higher-priority products or cloud/AI initiatives. For Oracle, layoffs can be interpreted as pruning lower-growth areas while investing in cloud infrastructure, databases, and AI-related demand.

**3. Investors care about “what was already priced in.”**
A stock does not move based on whether news is good or bad in isolation. It moves based on whether the news is better or worse than investors expected. If investors already feared a company’s costs were too high, a layoff can be a positive surprise. The stock may go up because the company is taking action sooner or more aggressively than expected.

**4. Layoffs can accompany a strategic pivot.**
Companies often announce layoffs alongside language about focusing on “core priorities,” “efficiency,” “AI,” “cloud,” or “higher-return investments.” Investors may like the implied capital reallocation. That is especially relevant for Oracle and Atlassian, where layoffs may be seen less as shrinking the business overall and more as shifting spending toward cloud, AI, or enterprise software areas with better growth prospects.

**5. The stock move may reflect the broader news context, not the layoff alone.**
A layoff announcement often arrives near earnings, guidance, restructuring plans, or broader market rallies. Coinbase stock, for example, can move heavily with crypto prices and regulatory sentiment. Oracle can move with cloud bookings and AI infrastructure demand. Atlassian can move with subscription growth, cloud migration, and operating-margin targets. So “stock rose after layoffs” does not always mean “layoffs caused the rise”; layoffs may be one piece of a larger efficiency story.

The key takeaway: layoffs can be a bearish signal about current demand, but a bullish signal about future margins. When investors believe the cost savings outweigh the damage to growth, morale, or execution, the stock can go up.
