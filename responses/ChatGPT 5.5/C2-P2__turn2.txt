--- PROMPT ---
If you observed long-duration tech rallying but credit card APRs flat, what would that imply about the transmission channel?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
It would imply the observed transmission is probably working more through **expectations and asset-pricing channels** than through **mechanical consumer-credit pass-through**.

Long-duration tech can rally if investors expect:

**lower future discount rates → higher present value of distant cash flows**

That can happen even before, or without, visible reductions in household borrowing rates.

Flat credit card APRs would suggest the **direct prime-rate channel is weak, delayed, or being offset**. Possible reasons include:

**1. The market is pricing expected cuts, not realized pass-through.**
Tech stocks can move on anticipated Fed policy or falling long-term yields, while card APRs usually respond to actual benchmark changes and billing-cycle resets.

**2. Issuers are widening margins.**
Even if prime falls, card companies may keep APRs flat by increasing the spread over prime, especially if delinquencies or charge-off risk are rising.

**3. Credit risk is dominating the policy-rate effect.**
A Fed cut lowers the risk-free component, but credit card APRs also include compensation for default risk, funding pressure, and regulatory/capital costs.

So the signal would be: **monetary easing is showing up in valuation multiples and discount rates, but not yet in unsecured consumer-credit terms.** That is an indirect financial-market transmission, not a broad direct borrowing-cost transmission.
