--- PROMPT ---
If you observed long-duration tech rallying but credit card APRs flat, what would that imply about the transmission channel?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
It would imply that the observed effect is coming mainly through the **expectations/asset-pricing channel**, not the **mechanical consumer-rate pass-through channel**.

Long-duration tech can rally because investors expect:

[
\text{lower future discount rates} \rightarrow \text{higher present value of future cash flows}
]

or because risk appetite improves. That can happen **before** actual loan rates adjust, and even if consumer APRs have not moved yet.

Flat credit card APRs would suggest one of three things:

1. **The Fed cut has not yet passed through to prime/APRs**, perhaps because of billing-cycle timing or lender adjustment lags.
2. **The rally is driven by expected future cuts**, not already-realized short-rate reductions.
3. **The tech rally is not mainly about lower borrowing costs**, but about lower long-term discount rates, lower risk premiums, better earnings expectations, or AI/growth optimism.

So the key inference is:

**Tech stocks are responding to market expectations and valuation discount rates, while credit card APRs are showing little evidence yet of direct pass-through to household borrowing costs.**

That means monetary transmission is visible first in **financial markets**, not yet in **consumer credit conditions**.
