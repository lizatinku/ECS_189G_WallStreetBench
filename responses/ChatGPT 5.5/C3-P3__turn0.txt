--- PROMPT ---
A major airline announces it will acquire a smaller regional carrier for $4.2 billion. The acquirer's stock drops 6% on the news while the target's stock jumps 22%. Explain why this asymmetric reaction is common in M&A and what it implies about how the market is pricing the deal.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
This reaction is common because the economics of an acquisition are usually split unevenly between the buyer and the seller.

For the **target**, a 22% jump usually means investors believe shareholders are receiving a takeover premium. In M&A, the buyer often pays more than the target’s unaffected market value to convince shareholders and the board to sell. So the target’s stock moves toward the implied offer price, adjusted for deal risk.

For the **acquirer**, a 6% drop can reflect several concerns:

The market may think the buyer is **overpaying**, especially if the offer price includes a large premium.

Investors may doubt the promised **synergies**, such as cost savings, route optimization, loyalty-program benefits, or fleet efficiencies.

The deal may create **integration risk**, which is especially relevant in airlines because labor contracts, fleet complexity, airport slots, union issues, IT systems, and regulatory approvals can be difficult.

The acquisition may increase **leverage or dilution**. If the buyer uses debt, the balance sheet becomes riskier; if it uses stock, existing shareholders may be diluted.

There may also be **regulatory risk**, particularly in airlines, where consolidation can raise antitrust and competition concerns.

The asymmetric reaction implies that the market is saying: “This is clearly valuable for the target’s shareholders, but less obviously value-creating for the acquirer’s shareholders.”

A useful way to read the stock moves is:

Target up 22% = market expects a meaningful premium and some probability the deal closes.

Acquirer down 6% = market is subtracting value from the buyer because it believes the purchase price, execution risk, financing burden, or regulatory uncertainty may outweigh the benefits.

So the market is not necessarily saying the deal will fail. It is saying the target shareholders are capturing much of the immediate deal value, while the buyer’s shareholders may be funding that value transfer and bearing the risk that the promised synergies do not materialize.
