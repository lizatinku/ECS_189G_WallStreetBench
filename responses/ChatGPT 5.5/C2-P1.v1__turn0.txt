--- PROMPT ---
U.S. 30-year mortgage rates went from roughly 3% in 2021 to a peak near 8% in late 2023 and now sit at about 6.37%. What does this mean for housing affordability and household demand for homes?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
It means **affordability is still much worse than in 2021, even though it has improved from the late-2023 peak**.

Freddie Mac’s 30-year fixed rate bottomed near **2.65% in January 2021**, peaked at **7.79% on Oct. 26, 2023**, and was recently around **6.37%–6.51% in May 2026**. ([Consumer Financial Protection Bureau][1])

For a **$400,000 mortgage**, principal-and-interest payment is roughly:

| Mortgage rate | Monthly payment |
| ------------: | --------------: |
|         3.00% |      **$1,686** |
|         6.37% |      **$2,494** |
|         8.00% |      **$2,935** |

So at **6.37%**, the payment is about **48% higher** than at 3%. At **8%**, it was about **74% higher**. Put differently, a household that could afford a **$400,000 loan at 3%** could only afford about a **$270,000 loan at 6.37%** with the same monthly payment.

For housing affordability, this does three things:

1. **Reduces purchasing power.** Buyers qualify for smaller loans, need larger incomes, or must buy cheaper/smaller homes.

2. **Weakens household demand for home purchases.** Some renters stay renters, some buyers delay purchasing, and others shift to cheaper regions or smaller homes. NAR has noted that even a 1 percentage-point drop in mortgage rates can add millions of households back into the pool of mortgage-qualified buyers, which shows how rate-sensitive demand is. ([National Association of REALTORS®][2])

3. **Creates “lock-in” among existing homeowners.** People with 3% mortgages are reluctant to sell and take a new 6%+ mortgage. That limits existing-home supply, which can keep prices from falling enough to fully restore affordability.

So the current situation is mixed: **6.37% is meaningfully better than 8%, so demand should recover somewhat from the 2023 freeze. But it is still far above 2021 levels, so affordability remains structurally tight and household demand is still suppressed compared with the low-rate era.** Recent housing data still show pressure from higher rates; for example, new single-family home sales fell in April 2026 amid elevated mortgage rates and prices. ([reuters.com][3])
