--- PROMPT ---
U.S. 30-year mortgage rates went from roughly 3% in 2021 to a peak near 8% in late 2023 and now sit at about 6.37%. What does this mean for housing affordability and household demand for homes?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
I can’t verify the latest 6.37% figure because web search is disabled here, but taking your rate path as given: the move from ~3% to ~6.37% is still a major affordability shock, even though it is easier than the late-2023 peak near 8%.

For a 30-year fixed mortgage, principal-and-interest payment per $100,000 borrowed is roughly:

| Mortgage rate | Monthly P&I per $100k | Change vs. 3% |
| ------------: | --------------------: | ------------: |
|         3.00% |                 ~$422 |             — |
|         6.37% |                 ~$624 |          +48% |
|         8.00% |                 ~$734 |          +74% |

So a buyer taking a $400,000 mortgage would pay about:

|  Rate | Monthly P&I |
| ----: | ----------: |
| 3.00% |     ~$1,686 |
| 6.37% |     ~$2,495 |
| 8.00% |     ~$2,935 |

That means affordability is still far worse than in 2021. Even at 6.37%, the same home loan costs about $800 more per month than it did at 3%. To keep the same payment a buyer could afford at 3%, the loan size at 6.37% would need to be roughly one-third smaller, assuming no change in taxes, insurance, or income.

For household demand, the effect is mixed:

Higher rates reduce “effective demand.” Many households may still want to buy, but fewer can qualify or are willing to pay today’s monthly cost. This weakens demand from first-time buyers and rate-sensitive move-up buyers.

The lock-in effect reduces both supply and turnover. Owners with 3% mortgages are reluctant to sell and take on a 6%–7% mortgage. That keeps existing-home inventory tighter than it otherwise would be, which can prevent home prices from falling enough to fully restore affordability.

Demand shifts toward rentals, smaller homes, cheaper metros, cash buyers, and new construction with builder incentives. Builders can offer rate buydowns, discounts, or smaller floor plans, so new-home demand may hold up better than existing-home sales.

The decline from 8% to 6.37% helps at the margin. It brings some buyers back, improves qualification ratios, and may revive pending sales. But it does not “normalize” affordability relative to 2021 because payments remain dramatically higher unless home prices or incomes adjust.

Bottom line: housing affordability is still deeply constrained, and household demand for homes is suppressed relative to the 2021 low-rate era. Lower rates from the 2023 peak should improve activity, but at ~6.37%, demand is likely to recover only gradually unless incomes rise, prices soften, or rates fall further.
