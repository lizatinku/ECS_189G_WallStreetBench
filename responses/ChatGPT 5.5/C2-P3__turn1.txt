--- PROMPT ---
Which assumption in your analysis is most fragile, and what would you do to stress-test it?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The most fragile assumption is **holding period**: the idea that the student will stay long enough for buying to overcome transaction costs and early mortgage-interest-heavy payments.

For a graduate student, this is especially uncertain because graduation, job placement, internships, visa/location constraints, relationship/family changes, or PhD/postdoc plans can force a move earlier than expected.

To stress-test it, I would run a break-even table across different exit dates:

| Scenario           | What to test                                               | Likely implication            |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Sell after 2 years | Closing costs + selling costs + little principal paid down | Buying likely loses           |
| Sell after 3 years | Some rent growth, but still high transaction cost burden   | Usually still risky           |
| Sell after 5 years | More rent inflation avoided, more equity built             | Buying may become competitive |
| Keep 7+ years      | Rent keeps compounding, mortgage fixed                     | Buying becomes much stronger  |

Then I’d add pessimistic assumptions:

* Condo value falls **5–10%**
* Rent growth is only **1–2%**, not 4%
* HOA rises or special assessment appears
* No refinance opportunity
* Student must sell quickly after graduation

The key stress test is:

**“If I had to move in 2–3 years, would buying still be tolerable, or would it create a financial problem?”**

If the answer is “that would be painful,” renting is probably the safer choice. If the student can either stay long-term or rent out the condo after moving, then buying becomes much less fragile.
