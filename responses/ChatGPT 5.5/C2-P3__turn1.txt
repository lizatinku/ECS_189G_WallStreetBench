--- PROMPT ---
Which assumption in your analysis is most fragile, and what would you do to stress-test it?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
The most fragile assumption is **how long the student will actually stay**.

That assumption drives almost everything else because buying has high fixed entry and exit costs. A condo can look attractive over 7–10 years but unattractive over 2–3 years, even with the same mortgage rate and rent growth.

I would stress-test it by running the rent-vs-buy comparison under several holding periods:

| Holding period | Likely effect                                                     |
| -------------: | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
|        2 years | Strongly favors renting unless condo appreciates unusually fast   |
|      3–4 years | Usually still risky for buying because transaction costs dominate |
|      5–7 years | Break-even zone; appreciation, HOA, and rent growth matter a lot  |
|    8–10+ years | Buying becomes more plausible if ownership costs are controlled   |

Then I would add a second stress test: assume the student is forced to sell at a bad time.

For example:

* Home prices flat for 5 years
* Home prices fall 5–10%
* HOA rises 5–8% annually
* One special assessment of $5,000–$15,000
* Sale costs of roughly 5–7% of the condo value
* Student moves after year 3 instead of year 6

The question I’d ask is:

**Can the buying case still survive if the student leaves earlier than planned and the condo does not appreciate?**

If the answer is no, then the “I’ll stay long enough” assumption is carrying the whole analysis. For a graduate student, that is especially fragile because graduation, job market outcomes, relationships, internships, funding, and location preferences can change quickly.
