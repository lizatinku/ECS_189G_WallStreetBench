--- PROMPT ---
Which of these three pass-throughs typically lags the most, and why?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
**Auto loan rates typically lag the most.**

Reason: their link to Fed cuts is **indirect and filtered through many pricing layers**.

Fed cut → lower short-term funding costs → possible easing in bank/finance-company funding → auto-loan pricing decisions → dealer/manufacturer incentives → borrower-specific offered rate.

That chain can take time, and it can be offset by other factors: credit spreads, default risk, used-car prices, loan term, borrower credit score, lender competition, and whether manufacturers are subsidizing rates.

By contrast:

**Credit card APRs** usually adjust faster because many are variable-rate products tied to the **prime rate**, which moves closely with the Fed funds rate. There may be a billing-cycle delay, but the formula is fairly direct.

**Long-duration tech stocks** may react fastest in market prices because stocks trade on **expectations**, not just actual rate changes. If investors expect cuts, tech valuations can move before the Fed cuts at all. But the effect is not guaranteed because earnings expectations and risk appetite may move at the same time.

So the rough lag ranking is:

**Tech stocks: often immediate/anticipatory**
**Credit card APRs: relatively fast, formula-driven**
**Auto loan rates: slowest and least complete pass-through**

For existing fixed-rate auto loans, the pass-through is basically **zero unless the borrower refinances**.
