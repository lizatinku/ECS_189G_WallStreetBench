--- PROMPT ---
Which of these three pass-throughs typically lags the most, and why?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
**Auto loan rates typically lag the most.**

Credit card APRs usually adjust relatively quickly because many are variable-rate products linked to the **prime rate**, which closely follows the Fed’s policy rate. Long-duration tech stocks can reprice almost immediately because public equity markets continuously update expectations about future rates, growth, and risk appetite.

Auto loans are slower because the pass-through is less mechanical. Lenders price them off a mix of funding costs, Treasury yields, securitization conditions, credit spreads, dealer incentives, borrower risk, loan terms, and competition. Since most auto loans are fixed-rate loans originated at the time of purchase, existing borrowers usually do not see their rate change; only new loans gradually reflect easier monetary conditions.

So the usual lag ranking is:

**Fastest:** long-duration tech stocks
**Next:** credit card APRs
**Slowest:** auto loan rates

The key distinction is that equities reprice on expectations, credit cards often follow a direct formula, and auto loans depend on slower-moving lender pricing and credit-market conditions.
