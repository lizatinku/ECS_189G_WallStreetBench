--- PROMPT ---
Airlines and trucking both pay for fuel — why might their stock reactions differ in magnitude and timing?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Airlines and trucking both face higher fuel costs, but their stocks can react differently because the **business models pass fuel costs through at different speeds and with different credibility**.

## Airlines: often larger, faster stock reaction

Airlines usually react more sharply because fuel is a very visible, large operating cost, and investors immediately worry about margin compression. Even when airlines can raise fares, demand is discretionary: higher ticket prices may reduce travel demand, especially leisure travel.

Airlines may also have fuel hedges, but hedge coverage varies widely. If investors think an airline is under-hedged, has weak pricing power, or is already facing soft demand, the stock may fall quickly after an oil spike.

**Timing:** often immediate in share prices; earnings impact can show within weeks to a quarter as jet fuel costs flow through.

## Trucking: reaction can be more uneven and delayed

Many trucking companies use **fuel surcharge mechanisms**, especially in contract freight. That means part of the fuel increase can be passed to customers. The pass-through is not always perfect, but it can soften the margin hit.

The timing also depends on the freight mix. Large contract carriers may recover fuel costs with a lag. Spot-market truckers or smaller operators may feel pressure faster because they have less bargaining power and less formal surcharge protection.

**Timing:** fuel costs hit immediately, but stock reaction may be slower or smaller if investors expect surcharges to offset the increase. The margin effect often appears over weeks to months.

## Why the magnitude differs

Airlines tend to be more sensitive to oil shocks because higher fuel prices can hurt them twice: first through costs, then through weaker travel demand if fares rise. Trucking is more directly tied to economic activity and contract structures, so the effect depends more on surcharge recovery, freight demand, and customer contracts.

So the simplified comparison is:

**Airlines:** bigger immediate market reaction because fuel hurts margins and fare increases can weaken demand.
**Trucking:** more mixed reaction because fuel surcharges can offset costs, but with delays and uneven coverage.
