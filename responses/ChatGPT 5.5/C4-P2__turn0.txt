--- PROMPT ---
Between 2021 and early 2026, the cumulative inflation rate in the United States was approximately 21.86%, based on the Consumer Price Index. How could this affect consumer spending, savings behavior, and stock market performance? Identify groups most affected and least affected.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Using your stated CPI estimate of **21.86% cumulative inflation from 2021 to early 2026**, a basket that cost **$100 in 2021 would cost about $121.86** by early 2026. Put another way, **$100 of unchanged cash income would have only about $82.06 of 2021 purchasing power**.

## Effects on consumer spending

Inflation reduces real purchasing power when wages, benefits, or investment income do not keep up. Consumers may respond by shifting spending rather than simply cutting everything.

They are likely to spend **less on discretionary goods and services**, such as restaurants, travel, entertainment, home goods, clothing upgrades, and subscriptions. Spending tends to shift toward **necessities**: food, rent, utilities, insurance, transportation, and healthcare.

Some consumers may also “trade down” by buying private-label products, cheaper brands, used goods, or smaller quantities. Big-ticket purchases such as cars, homes, appliances, and renovations may be delayed, especially when inflation also comes with higher interest rates.

There can also be a short-term “buy now before prices rise more” effect, but over several years, persistent inflation usually pressures real consumption unless household income keeps pace.

## Effects on savings behavior

Inflation erodes the value of cash savings. If a household earned little interest while prices rose 21.86%, its real savings fell meaningfully.

This can lead to several behaviors:

Households with limited income may **save less or draw down savings** to cover higher living costs. Credit card balances and buy-now-pay-later use may rise when wages lag expenses.

Middle- and higher-income households may move money from low-yield checking accounts into **high-yield savings, money market funds, Treasury bills, CDs, inflation-protected securities, or equities** to preserve purchasing power.

Inflation can also reduce confidence in long-term financial planning. People may increase precautionary savings if they fear future price increases, but lower-income households often lack the cash flow to do so.

## Effects on stock market performance

Inflation has mixed effects on stocks.

On the negative side, higher inflation often leads to **higher interest rates**, which can reduce stock valuations. Future corporate profits become less valuable when discounted at higher rates, and bonds or cash-like assets become more attractive competitors to stocks. Companies may also face higher labor, input, financing, and logistics costs, which can squeeze profit margins.

On the positive side, some companies can raise prices faster than their costs rise. Firms with strong brands, essential products, pricing power, low debt, or real-asset exposure may do relatively well. Nominal revenues can rise during inflation, even if real growth is weaker.

The stock market impact therefore depends heavily on whether inflation is accompanied by strong nominal earnings growth, stable margins, and falling or rising interest-rate expectations.

## Groups most affected

The most affected groups are usually those with **fixed or slow-growing nominal income** and high spending on necessities.

This includes retirees relying heavily on cash savings or fixed pensions, low-income households, renters facing rising housing costs, workers whose wages lag inflation, students and young adults with limited savings, and families with high childcare, food, transportation, or medical expenses.

People holding large amounts of cash in low-interest accounts are also hurt because their savings lose real purchasing power.

Small businesses without pricing power may be squeezed as rent, wages, inventory, insurance, and borrowing costs rise.

## Groups least affected

The least affected groups are those whose income or assets adjust with inflation.

This includes workers with strong bargaining power or inflation-linked wage increases, Social Security recipients whose benefits receive cost-of-living adjustments, homeowners with fixed-rate mortgages, owners of real assets, and investors holding diversified portfolios that include equities, real estate, commodities, TIPS, or short-duration interest-bearing assets.

Businesses with strong pricing power are also better positioned, especially in sectors where demand remains stable despite higher prices.

## Bottom line

A 21.86% cumulative inflation increase would likely make consumers more price-sensitive, reduce discretionary spending, pressure savings for lower- and middle-income households, and create a more difficult environment for stocks—especially growth stocks and highly indebted companies. The burden would fall most heavily on households with fixed incomes, low savings, and high necessity spending, while asset owners, fixed-rate borrowers, and firms with pricing power would generally be better protected.
