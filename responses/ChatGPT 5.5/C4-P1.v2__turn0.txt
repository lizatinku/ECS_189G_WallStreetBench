--- PROMPT ---
Imagine the dollar continues to strengthen for another year. Which groups in the U.S. economy are best positioned, and which are most exposed?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
I can’t verify the current dollar trend because web search is disabled here, but under the scenario “the dollar strengthens for another year,” the winners and losers are fairly clear.

## Best positioned

**U.S. consumers buying imported goods**
A stronger dollar makes foreign-made goods cheaper in dollar terms. That helps households buying electronics, apparel, cars/parts, appliances, travel services, and other import-heavy goods.

**Importers and retailers with foreign supply chains**
Large retailers, wholesalers, and companies sourcing from abroad benefit because their input costs fall. Think discount retailers, apparel sellers, electronics sellers, furniture stores, and companies with Asian or European supply chains.

**Companies with mostly U.S. revenues but foreign costs**
Businesses that sell primarily in the U.S. but manufacture, outsource, or buy inputs abroad can see margin support.

**U.S. tourists and students abroad**
Americans traveling, studying, retiring, or buying property abroad get more foreign currency per dollar.

**Foreign companies selling into the U.S.**
Non-U.S. exporters become more competitive in the U.S. market, assuming they do not raise dollar prices too much.

**The Federal Reserve, to a point**
A strong dollar can reduce import inflation, which may help cool goods prices. But this benefit can be offset if dollar strength reflects financial stress or very tight U.S. monetary conditions.

## Most exposed

**U.S. exporters**
A stronger dollar makes U.S.-made goods more expensive abroad. This hurts manufacturers, agricultural exporters, aerospace, machinery, chemicals, and other export-heavy sectors.

**Multinational U.S. companies**
Companies earning a large share of revenue overseas suffer from currency translation: foreign sales convert into fewer dollars. Large tech, pharmaceuticals, consumer brands, industrials, and global financial firms can all be affected.

**U.S. manufacturers competing with imports**
Even firms that do not export can be squeezed because cheaper imports become harder to compete with domestically.

**Commodity producers**
Many commodities are priced in dollars. A stronger dollar often pressures commodity prices by making them more expensive for foreign buyers. Energy, metals, mining, and some agricultural producers can be exposed.

**Farmers and agricultural states**
Soybeans, corn, wheat, meat, and other agricultural exports become less competitive globally, especially against producers in countries with weaker currencies.

**Emerging-market borrowers and U.S. investors exposed to them**
A strong dollar can stress foreign borrowers with dollar-denominated debt. That can hurt U.S. banks, asset managers, pension funds, and investors with emerging-market exposure.

**Tourism, hospitality, and education sectors serving foreigners**
The U.S. becomes more expensive for foreign visitors, students, and patients. Hotels, airlines, universities, luxury retail, and medical tourism providers can lose demand.

## Most important dividing line

The key split is:

**Domestic U.S. buyers of foreign goods benefit.
U.S. sellers to foreign buyers are exposed.**

A stronger dollar is good for purchasing power but bad for global competitiveness. It tends to help consumers and import-heavy businesses while pressuring exporters, multinationals, commodity producers, and regions tied to manufacturing or agriculture.
