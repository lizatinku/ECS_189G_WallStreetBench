--- PROMPT ---
Imagine the dollar continues to strengthen for another year. Which groups in the U.S. economy are best positioned, and which are most exposed?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
If the **dollar strengthens for another year**, the main divide is: **groups earning/spending in dollars and buying from abroad benefit; groups selling abroad or translating foreign earnings back into dollars suffer.**

## Best positioned

**1. U.S. consumers buying imported goods**
A stronger dollar makes foreign goods cheaper in dollar terms, so imported electronics, apparel, cars/parts, travel goods, and some food inputs become less expensive. The inflation benefit is real but not one-for-one because firms may keep margins and tariffs can offset it. Fed research finds exchange-rate pass-through to U.S. import prices is limited but meaningful. ([Federal Reserve][1])

**2. Import-heavy retailers and distributors**
Retailers that source abroad and sell mostly to U.S. consumers are relatively well positioned: Walmart-style general merchandise, apparel retailers, furniture, consumer electronics sellers, auto-parts distributors, and firms with foreign suppliers. Their costs fall in dollar terms, especially if they can avoid passing all savings to consumers.

**3. Domestic-service businesses**
Restaurants, healthcare, utilities, local services, domestic telecom, many regional banks, and U.S.-focused software firms are less directly hurt because their revenues and costs are mostly in dollars. They may also benefit indirectly if imported inputs are cheaper.

**4. U.S. outbound travelers and firms buying foreign assets**
Americans traveling abroad get more foreign currency per dollar. U.S. companies acquiring foreign assets or paying overseas labor/rent in local currency also gain purchasing power.

**5. Dollar-based financial intermediaries, with caveats**
If dollar strength reflects strong U.S. growth or relatively high U.S. rates, global capital may keep flowing into dollar assets. That can support Treasuries, U.S. money-market funds, and dollar funding franchises. But this becomes riskier if dollar strength is caused by global stress.

## Most exposed

**1. U.S. exporters**
Manufacturers, agriculture, aerospace, machinery, chemicals, and some energy/materials exporters become less competitive because their goods are more expensive for foreign buyers. The U.S. still has large two-way trade exposure: in March 2026, exports were $320.9 billion and imports were $381.2 billion, so currency moves matter for both sides. ([Bureau of Economic Analysis][2])

**2. Multinational companies with large foreign revenue**
A stronger dollar reduces the dollar value of overseas sales and profits when translated back into U.S. financial statements. It can also make U.S.-produced goods/services less price-competitive abroad. This especially matters for large-cap tech, pharmaceuticals, industrials, consumer brands, and semiconductors with big non-U.S. sales. FactSet has noted that S&P 500 firms with majority foreign sales had weaker expected earnings growth than more U.S.-domestic firms in early 2025. ([FactSet Insight][3])

**3. U.S. tourism, universities, and export-like services**
A strong dollar makes the U.S. more expensive for foreign tourists, international students, and foreign buyers of U.S. professional services. Hotels, airlines, luxury retail in tourist-heavy cities, and universities reliant on international tuition can be exposed.

**4. Domestic firms competing with imports**
Even companies that sell only in the U.S. can be hurt if foreign competitors suddenly become cheaper. This pressure can hit textiles, furniture, low-end manufacturing, auto parts, appliances, and some food producers.

**5. U.S. investors holding unhedged foreign assets**
Foreign stocks or bonds may perform fine in local currency, but gains can be reduced or erased when converted back into stronger dollars. This hurts U.S. pension funds, endowments, and households with unhedged international exposure.

**6. Emerging-market borrowers and commodity-linked firms, indirectly**
This is not purely “inside” the U.S. economy, but it feeds back through banks, investors, exporters, and commodity demand. IMF research finds U.S. dollar appreciations tend to create larger negative spillovers for emerging markets, especially commodity exporters. ([IMF][4])

## Bottom line

**Winners:** U.S. consumers, importers, U.S.-focused retailers, domestic-service firms, outbound travelers, and dollar-cash investors.
**Losers:** exporters, multinationals with foreign revenue, inbound tourism/education, import-competing manufacturers, unhedged foreign-asset investors, and firms exposed to emerging-market stress.

The key question is **why** the dollar is strengthening. If it is because the U.S. economy is outperforming, the damage is concentrated in exporters and multinationals. If it is because of global risk aversion, the pain broadens through credit stress, weaker foreign demand, and lower global growth.
