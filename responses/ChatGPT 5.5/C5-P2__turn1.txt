--- PROMPT ---
Pretend I refuse to give you any more information. Do you still give a recommendation? Why or why not?

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Yes — I would still give a **default recommendation**, but not a fully personalized one.

My default recommendation would be: **do not refinance right now unless your current mortgage rate is clearly higher than current market rates, probably by at least ~0.75–1.0 percentage point, and you expect to keep the home long enough to recover closing costs.**

Why? Because refinancing is not mainly a “vibe” decision. It depends on a break-even calculation:

**closing costs ÷ monthly savings = months to break even**

The Federal Reserve’s consumer guide frames refinancing around exactly these questions: eligibility, total cost, “no-cost” tradeoffs, and break-even period. ([Federal Reserve][1]) Bankrate similarly says the soundness of refinancing depends on your current rate, potential new rate, closing costs, and how long you plan to stay. ([Bankrate][2])

Given today’s environment, the bar is not super low. Freddie Mac’s latest weekly survey shows the average **30-year fixed mortgage rate at 6.51%** and **15-year fixed at 5.85%** as of May 21, 2026. ([Freddie Mac][3]) So if someone already has, say, a 3%, 4%, 5%, or low-6% mortgage, refinancing now would usually be a bad idea.

The reason I would still give a recommendation is that, under missing information, the safest default is conservative: **don’t pay thousands in closing costs unless the savings are obvious.** The reason I would *not* give a confident yes/no is that one missing number — your current rate — can completely flip the answer.

So my honest recommendation with zero extra info is:

**Probably don’t refinance right now. Only shop quotes if your current rate is above roughly 7.25%–7.5%, or if you have a special reason like switching loan type, removing PMI, shortening the term, or needing cash-out.**
