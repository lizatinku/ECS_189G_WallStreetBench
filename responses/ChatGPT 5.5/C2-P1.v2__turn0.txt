--- PROMPT ---
Mortgage rates are still more than double their 2021 lows. Discuss the implications for first-time home buyers, existing homeowners, and the broader housing market.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
As of Freddie Mac’s latest PMMS data, the 30-year fixed mortgage rate was **6.51% on May 21, 2026**, versus **2.65% on Jan. 7, 2021**—about **2.5 times higher**. On a $400,000 30-year mortgage, that difference raises principal-and-interest from about **$1,612/month** to **$2,531/month**, before taxes and insurance. ([Freddie Mac][1])

## First-time home buyers

For first-time buyers, the biggest effect is **affordability compression**. Higher rates reduce how much house a buyer can qualify for at the same income, so buyers either need a higher income, a larger down payment, a cheaper home, or must accept a much higher monthly payment. This is especially painful because home prices have not fallen enough to offset rates: NAR reported an April 2026 median existing-home price of about **$417,700**, still up year over year. ([National Association of REALTORS®][2])

It also changes buyer behavior. First-time buyers may delay purchasing, rent longer, buy farther from job centers, choose smaller homes, use rate buydowns, or consider adjustable-rate mortgages. The slight upside is that the market is less frantic than the 2020–2021 period: inventory has improved somewhat and days on market are longer, giving some buyers more room to negotiate. But that benefit is uneven because inventory still remains tight in many areas. ([National Association of REALTORS®][3])

## Existing homeowners

Existing homeowners with low fixed-rate mortgages are in a much stronger position **if they stay put**. A 3% mortgage is now almost like a financial asset: selling the home often means giving up a cheap loan and replacing it with a much more expensive one. This creates the “lock-in effect.” Freddie Mac describes this as a major factor limiting homes available for sale, and FHFA research found that the probability of sale falls sharply when current market rates exceed a homeowner’s original mortgage rate. ([Freddie Mac][4])

But homeowners are also constrained. People who want to move for a job, a larger home, downsizing, divorce, school district, or family reasons may stay in homes that no longer fit their needs. Refinancing is also much less attractive: instead of refinancing to lower payments, many owners are avoiding refinancing unless they need cash or have another strong reason.

## Broader housing market

The broader market becomes **frozen rather than simply weak**. Higher rates reduce demand, but low-rate homeowners also reduce supply by not listing. That combination can keep transaction volume low while preventing large national price declines. NAR reported April 2026 existing-home sales at a seasonally adjusted annual rate of **4.02 million**, basically flat year over year, while inventory was **1.47 million units**, or **4.4 months’ supply**—better than the extreme shortage period, but still not a fully loose market. ([National Association of REALTORS®][2])

New construction is affected differently. Builders may offer incentives or mortgage-rate buydowns, so new homes can become relatively more attractive than existing homes. But high rates also weaken sales and make builders cautious: Reuters reported that April 2026 new single-family home sales fell **6.2%** month over month and **11.3%** year over year, while new-home supply rose to **9.4 months**. ([Reuters][5])

Overall, elevated rates redistribute power: **cash buyers and high-income buyers gain relative advantage**, first-time buyers face a higher barrier to entry, existing low-rate homeowners stay protected but locked in, and the market sees fewer transactions, slower mobility, and more regional divergence. If rates fall meaningfully, demand may rebound—but so could competition, so affordability may not improve as much as buyers hope.
