--- PROMPT ---
Mortgage interest rates have increased by over 3 percentage points from early-2021 lows, peaking near 8% in late 2023 and sitting around 6.37% as of May 2026. How could this affect housing demand, monthly mortgage payments, and home affordability? Discuss any uncertainty or additional factors that may influence the outcome.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Higher mortgage rates mainly work like a **negative demand shock** for housing: they do not directly make homes less useful, but they make financing much more expensive.

Using a **$400,000, 30-year fixed mortgage** as an example:

| Mortgage rate | Monthly principal & interest |  Change vs. 2.65% |
| ------------: | ---------------------------: | ----------------: |
|         2.65% |             about **$1,612** |                 — |
|         6.37% |             about **$2,494** |   **+$882/month** |
|         7.79% |             about **$2,877** | **+$1,265/month** |

Freddie Mac reported the 30-year fixed rate at **2.65% in January 2021**, **7.79% in October 2023**, and **6.37% on May 7, 2026**; by May 21, 2026 it was **6.51%**. ([Freddie Mac][1]) The CFPB similarly estimates that the payment on a $400,000 loan rose by about **$1,265** from the 2021 trough to the 2023 peak. ([Consumer Financial Protection Bureau][2])

**Housing demand:** Higher rates reduce the number of buyers who qualify for mortgages and lower the amount many buyers can bid. Some households delay buying, keep renting, choose smaller homes, move to cheaper areas, or wait for rates to fall. This weakens transaction volume. For example, Reuters reported that April 2026 new single-family home sales fell **6.2%** and were down **11.3% year over year**, with elevated mortgage rates cited as a constraint. ([Reuters][3])

**Monthly payments:** The effect is large because mortgages are long-term loans. Even if the home price is unchanged, a move from roughly **2.65% to 6.37%** raises the monthly principal-and-interest payment by roughly **55%** on the same loan size. That means a buyer who could afford a $400,000 loan at 2.65% could afford only about **$258,500** at 6.37% if they wanted to keep the same monthly payment.

**Affordability:** Affordability worsens because buyers face both higher financing costs and still-high home prices. NAR reported an April 2026 existing-home median sales price of **$417,800**, with only **4.4 months of inventory**, so lower demand has not necessarily translated into a large price correction everywhere. ([National Association of REALTORS®][4]) Higher rates can also create a “lock-in effect”: homeowners with very low pandemic-era mortgages may avoid selling, which restricts supply and can keep prices elevated. ([Consumer Financial Protection Bureau][2])

The outcome is uncertain because mortgage rates are only one part of the market. Strong income growth, falling home prices, more inventory, builder incentives, mortgage-rate buydowns, or lower future rates could support demand. But weak employment, tighter credit, higher property taxes and insurance, persistent inflation, or higher Treasury yields could make affordability worse. The effect will also vary by region: expensive, supply-constrained markets may stay unaffordable, while areas with rising inventory may see more price reductions.
