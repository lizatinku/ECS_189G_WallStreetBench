=== Prompt ID: C2-P1  |  Category: C2  |  Turn: 0 ===

--- PROMPT ---
Mortgage interest rates have increased by over 3 percentage points from early-2021 lows, peaking near 8% in late 2023 and sitting around 6.37% as of May 2026. How could this affect housing demand, monthly mortgage payments, and home affordability? Discuss any uncertainty or additional factors that may influence the outcome.

--- MODEL RESPONSE ---
Using your rate assumptions, the main effect is a large **payment shock** that weakens demand and affordability unless incomes rise, prices fall, or credit terms loosen.

### 1. Housing demand likely weakens

Higher mortgage rates reduce the number of households that can qualify for a loan and make buying less attractive relative to renting or waiting. A move from roughly 3% mortgage rates to 6.37% means buyers face much higher monthly payments for the same home price, so many will lower their budgets, delay purchases, or exit the market.

There is also a “lock-in” effect: owners with 2.5% to 4% mortgages may avoid selling because buying another home would mean taking on a much higher rate. That can reduce both demand and supply. Fewer listings can partly offset weaker demand by keeping inventory tight and preventing prices from falling as much as affordability models alone would suggest.

### 2. Monthly mortgage payments rise sharply

For a 30-year fixed mortgage, principal and interest per **$100,000 borrowed** would be approximately:

| Mortgage rate | Monthly P&I per $100k |
| ------------: | --------------------: |
|         3.00% |                  $422 |
|         6.37% |                  $624 |
|         8.00% |                  $734 |

So for a **$400,000 mortgage**:

| Mortgage rate |  Monthly P&I |
| ------------: | -----------: |
|         3.00% | about $1,686 |
|         6.37% | about $2,494 |
|         8.00% | about $2,935 |

At 6.37%, the payment on the same $400,000 loan is about **48% higher** than at 3%. At 8%, it is about **74% higher**.

Another way to see the affordability hit: a buyer who could afford a $400,000 loan at 3% could afford only about **$270,000** at 6.37% for the same principal-and-interest payment, assuming the same 30-year term. At 8%, that falls to about **$230,000**.

### 3. Home affordability deteriorates

Affordability depends on home prices, mortgage rates, income, insurance, taxes, and down payments. Higher rates worsen affordability because more of a buyer’s income goes to interest rather than principal.

For example, even if the home price does not change, the buyer’s required income rises materially because lenders look at debt-to-income ratios. If home prices remain elevated while rates rise, affordability falls especially hard. This can price out first-time buyers, reduce bidding wars, and shift demand toward smaller homes, cheaper metros, condos, or rentals.

### 4. But the outcome is uncertain

The impact is not mechanical because several factors can offset or amplify higher rates:

Home prices may adjust downward in weaker markets, partly restoring affordability. But in supply-constrained markets, prices may stay firm despite higher rates.

Income growth can absorb some payment shock. If wages rise faster than expected, demand may be more resilient.

Inventory matters a lot. Low supply can keep prices high even when demand falls. More new construction or more existing-home listings could put downward pressure on prices.

Credit conditions matter. Tighter lending standards would further reduce demand; looser standards, buydowns, adjustable-rate mortgages, or seller concessions could support demand.

Expectations matter. If buyers believe rates will fall, they may buy now and hope to refinance later. If they believe rates will stay high or prices will fall, they may wait.

Regional differences are large. High-cost, rate-sensitive markets may see bigger demand declines, while areas with strong job growth, population inflows, or limited housing supply may hold up better.

Overall, a rise from early-2021 lows to around 6.37% would normally **reduce housing demand, raise monthly payments substantially, and worsen affordability**, but the final effect on prices and sales depends heavily on inventory, income growth, local labor markets, buyer psychology, and whether rates are expected to fall.
